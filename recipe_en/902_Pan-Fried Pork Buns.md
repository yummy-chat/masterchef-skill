# Pan-Fried Pork Buns

Cuisine: Shanghai Local Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Winter, All-Season Meal

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🧅 2 stalks of small scallions (for making scallion oil)  
🧅 About 2 slices of onion (for making scallion oil)  
🫚 About 20g ginger (for making ginger juice)  
🥩 500g pork hind leg meat (30% fat, 70% lean)  
🥣 60g meat aspic (prepared in advance and chilled until set)  
🌾 300g all-purpose flour  
🧪 3g Danbaoli yeast (high-activity dry yeast)  
🌽 21g corn oil (6g for kneading dough + 15g for making scallion oil)  
💧 Cold water, as needed (max 150g for kneading dough; 100g for ginger juice)  
🧅 Scallions, chopped, as needed (dip a little when wrapping filling)  
🌾 Sesame seeds, as needed (sprinkle on top before removing from pan)

🍬 6g granulated sugar (for kneading dough)  
🧂 6g salt (for mixing meat filling)  
🦪 20g oyster sauce  
🧴 14g light soy sauce  
🧴 6g dark soy sauce  
🌿 2g thirteen-spice powder  
🌿 1g white pepper powder  
🫒 5g sesame oil

## Kitchen Tools & Equipment
🍳 Meat grinder (with mixing function)  
🥣 KitchenAid stainless steel bowl (or regular mixing bowl)  
🍳 Small milk pot  
🍳 Non-stick frying pan  
🥣 Glass bowl  
🥖 Rolling pin  
🔪 Silicone scraper  
🥄 Sieve  
📦 Sealed bag

## Preparation Steps
**Preparatory Stage**:
1. The day before, boil the pork skin with water, strain, and refrigerate until set to make aspic. Set aside.
2. In a pan, add 15g corn oil, then add 2 stalks of small scallions and 2 slices of onion. Fry over low heat until the scallions turn golden. Remove from heat. While still hot, add 20g oyster sauce, 14g light soy sauce, 6g dark soy sauce, 6g granulated sugar, 2g thirteen-spice powder, and 1g white pepper powder. Stir well. Finally, add 5g sesame oil and let cool. Set aside.
3. Cut about 20g ginger and blend with 100g water in the meat grinder until fine. Strain to obtain ginger juice.
4. Place 500g pork, 6g salt, and the ginger juice into the meat grinder. Grind until a smooth, sticky meat paste forms. Transfer to a large bowl. Add the cooled scallion oil sauce and 60g of chopped meat aspic. Mix thoroughly by hand until sticky and thick enough that the bowl can be turned upside down without the filling falling out. Refrigerate until needed.
5. Place 300g all-purpose flour, 6g granulated sugar, and 3g yeast into the mixing bowl. Add no more than 150g cold water and 6g corn oil. Mix until no dry flour remains and it forms crumbs. Remove, knead until smooth, cover with plastic wrap, and refrigerate to rest for 1–2 hours.

**Cooking Stage**:
1. Take out the chilled dough, roll it into a long log, and divide it into pieces about 18g each. Dust with flour to prevent sticking.  
**Note**: The dough is in a semi-fermented state.
2. Add a suitable amount of oil to the bottom of a non-stick pan. Set aside.
3. Roll each dough piece into a round wrapper with a thicker center and thinner edges. Place meat filling in the center of the wrapper. For extra scallion flavor, dip a little raw chopped scallion onto the filling. Pleat the wrapper to form at least 12 folds using the standard bun-wrapping technique, and pinch the top tightly to seal.
4. Dust the bottom of each raw bun generously with flour. Place them with space in between into the oiled pan. Cover and let rest at room temperature for about 10 minutes to proof.
5. Heat the pan over medium heat. When the oil begins to bubble, pour hot water into the pan until it reaches half the height of the raw buns. Immediately cover with a lid, reduce to medium heat, and cook covered with steam for about 12 minutes.  
**Key point**: The lid must be put on immediately after adding water to trap the steam for cooking.
6. When the sound from the pan changes from a "gurgle" to a "crackle," remove the lid. Sprinkle with sesame seeds. Continue frying until the bottoms turn golden brown and crispy. Remove from the pan.  
**Judgment tip**: The change in sound indicates the water has evaporated, and the bottoms are beginning to crisp.

## Health Advice
Pan-fried pork buns are rich in high-quality protein and collagen. The meat aspic adds a rich texture, but the dish is relatively high in calories. It is recommended to pair with light vegetables for balanced nutrition. Suitable for those with high physical energy expenditure or as a winter tonic, but should be consumed in moderation by those with weak digestion.

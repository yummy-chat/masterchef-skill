# Sautéed Sliced Pork 
Cuisine: Northeast Chinese  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🥩 Pork loin or hind leg meat (with a little fat, cut into evenly sized cubes)  
🥔 Potato starch (about half the amount of the meat, soaked in cold water in advance until fully softened)  
🫑 Green bell peppers (moderate amount, broken into pieces)  
🧅 Scallions (chopped)  
🧄 Ginger (minced)  
🧄 Garlic (plenty, minced into pieces)  
🧂 Salt (about 1–2 g for marinating the meat, about 4 g for seasoning, overall saltiness on the lower side)  
🌊 Light soy sauce (moderate amount, for sizzling in the wok)  
🌑 Dark soy sauce (a few drops, for coloring)  
🍬 Sugar (a pinch to enhance freshness)  
🍶 Rice vinegar (moderate amount, poured along the edge of the wok just before removing from heat)  

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment  
🔪 Chef's knife and cutting board  
🥣 Stainless steel bowl (for soaking starch and mixing batter)  
🍳 Wok  
🌡️ Oil thermometer (or chopsticks to test oil temperature)  
🥄 Slotted spoon  
🥄 Spoon (for adjusting sauce and thickening)  

## Cooking Steps
1. **Soak the starch**: Add cold water to dry potato starch until submerged, let it sit for about 10 minutes. Do not stir during this time, allowing it to settle naturally.
2. **Cut the meat**: Thaw the frozen meat until it is semi-firm, then cut it into evenly sized cubes.
3. **Prepare the ingredients**: Break the green bell peppers into pieces; chop the scallions, mince the ginger; mince the garlic into pieces and set aside separately.
4. **Mix the batter**: Pour off the clear water on top of the soaked starch, retaining the wet starch paste at the bottom. Mix it with the meat cubes, adding small amounts of cold water gradually and stirring by hand.
   - The batter should have a yogurt-like consistency, with the meat color faintly visible, and must not be too thin.
   - Do not add oil to the batter to avoid excessive puffing during frying.
5. **Fry the meat cubes**: Heat the oil to 180–200°C. Add the meat cubes one by one, frying in batches.
   - Fry the first batch until set, then remove; fry the second batch until set, then return the first batch for combined frying.
   - Fry until the color is golden and the surface batter cracks open, revealing the meat. Achieve a crispy texture without needing a second fry.
   - If the meat cubes stick together, a gentle nudge after brief frying will separate them.
6. **Flash-fry the bell peppers**: While hot, place the bell peppers on a slotted spoon and pour hot oil over them or quickly pass them through the oil.
7. **Sauté and thicken the sauce**: Leave a small amount of oil in the wok, sizzle the chopped scallions and minced ginger, then drizzle in light soy sauce. Add a small amount of water and bring to a boil. Add salt, a pinch of sugar, and a few drops of dark soy sauce for color.
   - Once the sauce boils, stir in pre-soaked water starch to thicken. Continue heating until it boils vigorously, becoming thick and translucent.
   - The thickened sauce should coat a spoon, and the total amount of sauce should be small, ensuring a final "half-coated, half-not" state.
8. **Combine and serve**: Add the fried meat cubes and bell peppers, immediately sprinkle in all the minced garlic, and pour rice vinegar along the hot edge of the wok. Stir-fry quickly over high heat to mix evenly, then remove from heat immediately.
**Key point**: The vinegar added along the wok edge is the finishing touch. The minced garlic must be added last to highlight the garlic aroma and vinegar fragrance.

## Health Recommendations
This sautéed sliced pork dish involves a meticulous process where the batter does not include oil, and the final quick stir-fry over high heat effectively controls oil absorption, making it suitable for occasional consumption. Pork loin provides high-quality protein, while green bell peppers supplement vitamins, offering balanced nutrition. However, pay attention to salt control; it is advisable for those with high blood pressure to consume in small amounts. The use of vinegar along the wok edge can cut through greasiness and enhance flavor.

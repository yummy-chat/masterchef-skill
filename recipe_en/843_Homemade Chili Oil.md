# Homemade Chili Oil
Cuisine: Shaanxi Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🌶️ Coarse chili powder 120g (for smoky aroma)  
🌶️ Fine chili powder 160g (for vibrant red color)  
🧅 Scallion 50g (cut into sections)  
🧅 Onion 80g (julienned)  
🫚 Ginger 30g (sliced)  
🧈 Peeled white sesame seeds, to taste  
🫒 Rapeseed oil 1.5kg  
⭐ Star anise 4g  
🌿 Cinnamon stick 4g  
🌸 Sichuan peppercorns 5g  
🍃 Bay leaves 2g  
🌰 Black cardamom 4g  
🌻 Cumin seeds 6g  
🍶 Baijiu (Chinese liquor) 1 tablespoon (about 10-15ml, for cooling and preserving color)

## Cookware & Equipment
🍳 Wok or frying pan  
🥣 Heatproof large bowl  
🥄 Ladle or soup spoon  
🥢 Slotted spoon or strainer

## Instructions
1. Place the coarse chili powder and fine chili powder into separate containers and set aside. Cut the scallion into sections, julienne the onion, and slice the ginger. Rinse all spices, then soak them until moistened.
2. Pour 1.5kg of rapeseed oil into the wok. Heat over high heat until the oil is fully cooked through to remove the raw oil flavor, then turn off the heat and allow the oil to cool naturally to warm.
**Note**: Rapeseed oil must be fully heated to eliminate any raw, grassy taste.
3. When the oil temperature drops to about 120-140°C, add the scallion sections, onion shreds, and ginger slices, then add the moistened spices. Continue frying over low heat slowly.
**Key**: Adding ingredients at a low oil temperature prevents splattering; slow frying over low heat fully infuses the oil with the aromas of the vegetables and spices.
4. Fry until the vegetable pieces turn dry and golden and the spices release their fragrance. Use a slotted spoon to remove and discard all the solids, leaving only the fragrant oil in the wok.
5. Reheat the oil in the wok to 200°C. Ladle out about one-third of the hot oil and pour it in batches into the bowl containing the coarse chili powder, stirring continuously as you pour, until the coarse chili powder is thoroughly "bloomed" by the high heat, releasing a smoky, toasty aroma.
6. While the remaining oil in the wok is still at high temperature, sprinkle in the raw peeled white sesame seeds. The high heat will quickly toast them, and the sesame seeds will float evenly on the surface of the oil.
7. Allow the oil in the wok to naturally cool to about 165°C. Then, slowly sprinkle the fine chili powder into the oil in batches, stirring constantly to fully release the bright red color.
**Key**: 165°C is a medium-low temperature that ensures the fine chili powder yields a pure, vibrant red color; if the oil is too hot, the color will turn dark or black.
8. Immediately add one tablespoon of baijiu to the wok and stir quickly and evenly. The evaporation of the alcohol rapidly lowers the temperature, preventing residual heat from continuing to cook the oil and darkening the color.
9. Pour all the red oil from the wok into the large bowl containing the coarse chili powder, combining it with the earlier smoky chili mixture. Stir thoroughly.
**Purpose**: This merges the two flavors—"bloomed oil for smoky aroma" and "slow-heated oil for red color"—resulting in a smooth, bright red, complexly flavored final product.
10. Allow to cool completely before use. Suitable for mixing with noodles, stuffing into flatbreads, or dressing cold dishes.

## Health Advice
This chili oil is rich in vitamin E and capsaicin, which can stimulate appetite and promote blood circulation. It is ideal for adding fragrance to noodles or cold dishes. However, individuals with high blood pressure, high blood sugar, or high blood lipids should control their intake. During preparation, carefully manage the oil temperature in stages to avoid scorching and the formation of harmful substances.

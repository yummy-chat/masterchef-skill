# Sichuan-style Sesame Sauce Cold Noodles
Cuisine: Sichuan
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Summer (April-August)

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🍜 Fine round noodles, appropriate amount (suitable for cold noodles)
🌶️ Dried Sichuan peppercorns (Dahongpao), appropriate amount
🫘 White sesame seeds, toasted, appropriate amount
🥒 Cucumber, about 1 piece (shredded for the base)
🥬 Purple cabbage or Watermelon radish, small amount (shredded for garnish)
🧄 Garlic cloves, appropriate amount
🫚 Ginger, appropriate amount
🫒 Vegetable oil (neutral oil), appropriate amount
🫒 Sesame oil, small amount
⭐ Star anise, 1 piece
🧅 Scallion segments, appropriate amount
🫚 Ginger slices, appropriate amount
🌶️ Chili powder, appropriate amount
🧂 Salt, appropriate amount (added in batches)
🫘 Sesame paste, appropriate amount
💧 Cool boiled water, appropriate amount
🍬 White sugar, appropriate amount
🧂 MSG, appropriate amount
🍾 Soy sauce, appropriate amount (brought to a boil to make cooked soy sauce)
🍶 Rice vinegar, appropriate amount
🧅 Scallions, chopped, appropriate amount

## Cookware and Equipment
🍳 Wok or frying pan
♨️ Steamer
🍲 Large stockpot
🥄 Strainer or slotted spoon
🍳 Roasting pan or small flat pan
🪨 Mortar and pestle
🔪 Knife and cutting board
🍽️ Large round plate or tray
🌬️ Air conditioner or electric fan
🥣 Bowls, spoons, chopsticks
🥖 Rolling pin

## Preparation Steps
1. Pour vegetable oil and a small amount of sesame oil into a wok. Add scallion segments, ginger slices, 1 star anise, and a few Sichuan peppercorns. Fry slowly over low heat until the aromatics are dry, fragrant, and golden brown. Remove the aromatics, leaving the scallion oil behind.
**Note**: Must fry slowly over low heat to fully release the fragrance of the spices; the aromatics must be fried until golden brown to ensure the flavor is fully infused into the oil.
2. Place chili powder in a bowl. First, mix with a small amount of cold scallion oil to moisten. Heat the remaining scallion oil until smoking, then pour it over the chili powder in batches, stirring continuously. After the oil is incorporated, add one spoonful of salt and stir well.
**Note**: Moistening with cold oil prevents the chili powder from burning when hit with hot oil; the oil temperature must be high enough for fragrance; adding salt enhances the flavor.
3. Loosen the raw noodles and place them in a steamer. Steam over high heat for 9 minutes.
**Note**: Steaming is the first step to fully cook the noodles while maintaining a chewy texture.
4. Let the steamed noodles cool slightly, then plunge them into a pot of boiling water. As soon as the water returns to a boil, immediately remove the noodles and drain well.
**Note**: Steaming then boiling ensures the noodles are fully cooked with a tender yet firm texture, but do not boil for long.
5. Spread the drained hot noodles on a large plate or tray. Drizzle with an appropriate amount of the prepared scallion oil. Use chopsticks to toss and separate the noodles. Then place the noodles under a fan or air conditioner vent to cool until the surface is dry and no longer sticky.
**Note**: Applying oil while the noodles are hot helps absorption and prevents sticking; rapid air-drying is key for smooth, non-clumpy noodles.
6. Peel the garlic cloves. Add a pinch of salt and a small amount of cool boiled water, then crush into a paste. Add more cool boiled water to make garlic water. Finely chop the ginger, add a pinch of salt and cool boiled water, and crush into ginger juice.
7. In a clean, dry pan, toast the dried Sichuan peppercorns and white sesame seeds separately over low heat until fragrant. Add one spoonful of salt to the sesame seeds while toasting. Grind the toasted Sichuan peppercorns into a fine powder using a mortar and pestle. Crush the sesame seeds with a rolling pin and set aside.
8. Add cool boiled water to the sesame paste in batches, stirring in one direction until smooth and pourable. Bring the soy sauce to a boil and add it hot to the sesame paste in batches, stirring to combine. Then add white sugar, MSG, rice vinegar, ginger juice, garlic water, chopped scallions, ground Sichuan pepper powder, and the prepared chili oil. Stir thoroughly to create a complex sauce.
**Note**: When thinning sesame paste, add water little by little and stir in one direction to prevent it from separating; boiling the soy sauce before adding enhances the aroma of the sauce.
9. Line a plate with shredded cucumber. Pile the cooled noodles into a small mound on top of the cucumber shreds. Arrange shredded purple cabbage or watermelon radish around the noodles. Sprinkle the crushed toasted sesame seeds on top.
10. Drizzle the prepared sesame sauce evenly over the noodles. Before eating, toss the noodles and sauce thoroughly.

## Health Tips
Adding cucumber shreds to cold noodles increases dietary fiber. Sesame paste is rich in calcium but high in calories; it is recommended to consume at lunch and pair with a light soup. In summer, reduce the amount of chili oil to lessen irritation to the stomach.

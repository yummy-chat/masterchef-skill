# Braised Pork Hock with Rock Sugar
Cuisine: Common Home-Style Dish
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for All Seasons

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🐷 1 pork front hock (recommended for more lean meat and no bones on either side)
🍬 Rock sugar, as needed (for making sugar syrup)
🧂 Salt, about 8 grams (or adjust to taste after tasting the broth)
🥄 Light soy sauce, as needed (for enhancing flavor and added again when making sugar syrup)
🍶 Yellow wine, a small amount (for reducing gameyness and enhancing flavor)
🟤 Caramel color, as needed (for adjusting the broth color)
🍇 Aged vinegar or aromatic vinegar, a small amount (to add fragrance and reduce greasiness)
🫒 Sesame oil, a small amount (to enhance aroma and shine)
🌽 Water starch, as needed (for thickening)
🫚 Ginger slices, as needed
🧅 Scallion sections, as needed
⭐ Star anise, as needed

## Kitchenware and Equipment
🔥 Kitchen torch (for scorching the pork hock skin)
🍲 Stockpot (deep pot, for blanching and braising)
🍳 Wok (for making caramel color and sugar syrup)
🥄 Slotted spoon (for lifting the pork hock)
🔪 Chef's knife, cutting board (for scoring)
🥣 Large bowl/basin (for cleaning the pork hock)

## Preparation Steps
1. Use a kitchen torch to scorch the pork hock skin until blackened and charred, ensuring it is thoroughly burned to completely remove gland odor.
2. Wash the scorched pork hock with warm water and scrape off the charred parts.
3. Make a cut along the bone at the thickest part of the meat until you hit the bone, to allow blood to drain during blanching.
4. Place in cold water, ensuring the water covers the pork hock. After the water boils, cook for about 15 minutes until the surface changes color and is just cooked through.
5. Remove the pork hock and wash off any foam and impurities with warm water.
6. Place the pork hock in an old broth or fresh water, add ginger slices, scallion sections, star anise, light soy sauce, and yellow wine.
   - If using old broth, there is no need to bring it to a boil before adding the pork hock; add soy sauce and yellow wine in advance to reduce gameyness.
7. After it comes to a boil, reduce the heat to the lowest setting and braise for 2 hours, until a chopstick can easily pierce through.
8. Turn off the heat and let the pork hock soak in the broth for 4 to 16 hours to allow it to fully absorb the flavor.
   **Note**:
   - During soaking, ensure the skin of the pork hock is completely submerged in the broth; otherwise, exposed parts will become hard after cooling.
   - To maintain a whole and intact shape, wrap the pork hock in gauze or a cloth bag before braising.
9. Make the sugar syrup: Heat a small amount of peanut oil in a wok, add rock sugar, and stir-fry over medium to low heat.
   - Once the rock sugar melts and turns date-red, proceed immediately to the next step to prevent burning and bitterness.
10. Immediately add enough hot water to the wok.
11. Add light soy sauce and aged vinegar or aromatic vinegar, and bring to a boil to enhance aroma and reduce greasiness.
12. Gradually add water starch in batches to thicken, until the sauce becomes thick and forms dense small fish-eye bubbles.
    **Key point**: The sauce must be thoroughly cooked through; otherwise, it will thin out as it cools.
13. Plate the pork hock and pour the thick sugar syrup over it. For better flavor, you can first sprinkle shredded scallions on top and pour hot oil to release the aroma before adding the syrup.
14. The finished dish has a shiny red color, tender and falling-off-the-bone meat, savory with a hint of sweetness. Serve and enjoy while hot.

## Health Advice
Braised pork hock with rock sugar is rich in collagen, suitable for those needing protein supplementation and those interested in skin nourishment. However, it is high in calories, so those with high blood lipids should consume it in moderation. Ensure an adequate soaking time; otherwise, the skin may become hard and affect the texture.

# Homemade Tofu
Cuisine: Common Home-Style Dish
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for All Seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🫘 Dried soybeans 200g (need to be soaked in advance)
💧 Water 1000ml (for making soy milk) + 1500ml (for boiling soy milk) + 50ml (for mixing vinegar water)
🍾 White vinegar 50ml (about 3 hot pot soup spoons), diluted with water at a 1:1 ratio

## Kitchenware & Equipment
🍲 Stainless steel pot
🌀 Blender or food processor
🧻 Cheesecloth or sieve
🥟 Steamer rack
🥣 Large bowl or basin
🏋️ Heavy object (such as a bowl filled with water)
🥄 Wooden spoon or soup ladle
🥛 Measuring cup
🍳 Frying pan (for subsequent cooking)

## Steps
1. **Soak the soybeans**: Soak 200g of dried soybeans in water overnight until the beans are plump and significantly increased in volume.
2. **Prepare the vinegar water**: In a measuring cup, mix 50ml of white vinegar with 50ml of water and stir to combine.
   **Key point**: The vinegar must be diluted before use to avoid rapid curdling resulting in uneven coagulation.
3. **Blend the raw soy milk**: Drain the soaked soybeans, place them in the blender, add 1000ml of water, and blend until you have a smooth raw soy milk.
   **Consistency check**: It should be a uniform milky white liquid.
4. **Strain out the okara**: Place a damp cheesecloth over the pot, pour in the raw soy milk, gather the cheesecloth and squeeze out the milk, filtering out the okara.
   **Key point**: Squeeze as dry as possible to obtain more soy milk.
5. **Boil the soy milk**: Add 1500ml of water to the strained soy milk and stir. Heat over medium heat until it boils, then continue boiling for 5 minutes.
   **Consistency check**: A large amount of foam will appear on the surface of the soy milk.
   **Key point**: Stir continuously with a spoon while boiling, **do not leave the stove**, to prevent the soy milk from overflowing.
6. **Cool the soy milk**: Remove from heat and let it sit for about 2 minutes to cool down.
   **Key point**: Proper cooling helps with curdling.
7. **Curdle**: Skim off the skin that forms on the surface of the soy milk. Slowly drizzle the vinegar water into the soy milk one spoonful at a time while gently and slowly pushing the soy milk with a spoon to mix it evenly.
   **Consistency check**: The soy milk will gradually separate into white tofu curds and pale yellow whey.
   **Key point**: **This is the most critical step**. The action must be very gentle and slow. Avoid pouring all at once or stirring quickly, otherwise the texture of the tofu will be coarse like okara.
8. **Rest**: After curdling, cover or let it sit for 2 minutes to allow the tofu curds to solidify further.
9. **Strain and shape**: Line the steamer rack with cheesecloth and place a basin underneath. Pour the solidified curds and whey onto the cheesecloth. After some whey has drained, gather the edges of the cheesecloth to wrap the curds and press them flat with your hands.
10. **Press**: Place the wrapped curds into a mold and put a heavy object on top.
    **Time**: Press for 40 minutes for silken tofu, or about 1 hour for firm tofu.
    **Key point**: The pressing time determines the firmness; the cheesecloth needs to be pulled tight and wrapped well so the finished product doesn't fall apart.
11. **Completion and serving**: Remove the heavy object, open the cheesecloth, and take out the tofu block. It can be cut into pieces and used directly, or used for secondary cooking such as pan-frying, braising, or stewing.
    **Finished product characteristics**: Springy and smooth, not easily falling apart, and versatile.

## Health Tips
Homemade tofu is rich in plant protein and calcium, making it suitable for vegetarians and those needing to supplement their protein intake. Note that soy products can be slow to digest; those with sensitive stomachs should avoid eating excessive amounts.

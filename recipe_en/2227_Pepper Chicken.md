# Pepper Chicken
Cuisine: Cantonese
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Winter

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🍗 One whole chicken (recommended to choose one with a small beak, small wings, small feet, and a large rear)
🌶️ A small handful of red Sichuan peppercorns (distinctive aroma)
🌶️ A small handful of white peppercorns
🪵 A small handful of pepper roots (core ingredient, wash in advance, rich flavor)
🧄适量 Ginger (cut into pieces)
🧄适量 Garlic (minced)
🧅适量 Shallots (red onion, cut into pieces)
🧂适量 Fine salt
🍬适量 White sugar
🫗 About 2 tablespoons light soy sauce (once for marinating the chicken cavity, once for braising and drizzling)
🦪 About 1 tablespoon oyster sauce
🍶适量 Baijiu (to remove gameyness and enhance aroma, can be used to ignite flames)

## Cookware and Equipment
🍳 Frying pan
🍲 Wok
🥄 Ladle
🔒 Lid
🔪 Cutting board/knife
🔥 Portable gas stove

## Steps
1. Cut the ginger into pieces, mince the garlic, and cut the shallots into pieces. Wash the pepper roots. Mix the white and red Sichuan peppercorns together. Clean the chicken thoroughly and drain excess water.
2. Rub fine salt and white sugar evenly all over the chicken, inside and out. Pour about one tablespoon of light soy sauce into the chicken cavity; do not apply it to the outside. Marinate for 15 minutes.
**Note**: The light soy sauce mainly penetrates the cavity to add flavor; do not add it to the outside for now.
3. Heat the frying pan and add chicken fat. Place the marinated chicken skin-side down and fry over medium-low heat. Continuously scoop up hot oil with a ladle and pour it over the chicken until the skin turns golden brown.
**Key point**: Frying the chicken with chicken fat makes it more aromatic; patiently frying until golden directly affects the final color.
4. In a separate wok (no oil), add the mixed white and red Sichuan peppercorns. Stir-fry over medium-low heat for about 1 minute until a strong pepper aroma is released, then remove.
**Key point**: Dry-roasting fully releases the aroma of the peppercorns.
5. Heat oil in the wok. Add the minced garlic, ginger pieces, and shallots in order, then add the pepper roots. Stir-fry over medium heat until the aromatics are fragrant and slightly charred on the surface.
6. Place the fried chicken into the wok. Stuff some of the dry-roasted peppercorns into the chicken cavity and scatter the rest over the chicken and in the wok. Pour baijiu along the edge of the wok, ignite the flame to burn off the alcohol. Add hot water, then drizzle in one tablespoon of light soy sauce and one tablespoon of oyster sauce.
7. Cover the wok with a lid and braise over medium heat for 18 minutes, turning the chicken once halfway. The chicken is done when a chopstick can easily pierce the thickest part of the thigh and clear juices run out.
**Key point**: Igniting the baijiu adds flavor; steam allows the pepper aroma to fully infuse; the sauce thickens naturally from chicken gelatin, oyster sauce, and pepper roots—no need to thicken with starch.
8. Once braised, remove the chicken, chop it into pieces, and arrange on a plate. Reheat the sauce in the wok to reduce it further, then pour it over the chicken pieces.
**Key point**: The sauce thickens naturally; do not use a starch slurry.

## Health Advice
This pepper chicken uses a variety of peppercorns and is slow-fried in chicken fat. It warms the body and dispels cold, making it especially suitable for people with cold hands and feet in winter. However, those with high blood lipids should remove the skin before eating. Although igniting the baijiu during cooking enhances the flavor, ensure safety by keeping it away from flammable materials.

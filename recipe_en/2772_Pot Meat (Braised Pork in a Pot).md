# Pot Meat (Braised Pork in a Pot)

Cuisine: Northeast Chinese
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Winter (November-December)

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🥩 Pork belly, appropriate amount (alternating fat and lean layers, cut into about 1.5 cm pieces)
🧅 Scallion, appropriate amount (cut into sections)
🫚 Ginger, appropriate amount (lightly smashed)
🌿 Cilantro, appropriate amount (added in two batches, not too much)
🧊 Yellow rock sugar, appropriate amount (for caramelizing)
🌿 Cinnamon sticks, 2 pieces
⭐ Star anise, about 6-8 cloves (ensure authenticity to avoid consuming toxic star anise from *Illicium lanceolatum*)
🍶 Huadiao wine, appropriate amount (for blanching to remove odor)
🥄 Fermented bean curd sauce, appropriate amount
🥄 Sweet bean paste, appropriate amount
🧂 Dark soy sauce, appropriate amount
🧂 Salt, about half a spoon
🍬 Sugar, about 1 spoon

## Kitchenware and Equipment
🔪 Thick round cutting board
🔪 Chef's knife
🍳 Iron wok
🥄 Slotted spoon
🫙 Glass jar (or clay pot, for pot simmering)
🍲 Clay pot (for final serving)
🥣 Small bowl

## Steps
1. Place the pork belly on the cutting board. Use the chef's knife to scrape off any impurities on the skin's surface.
**Key point**: The skin must be thoroughly scraped and cleaned to remove surface dirt.
2. First cut the pork belly into strips, then into cubes about 1.5 cm per side, ensuring all pieces are roughly uniform in size.
**Key point**: The pieces should not be too large. Pieces around 1.5 cm are easier to flavor and have better texture after cooking.
3. Add cold water to the wok. Add the pork belly pieces. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to medium heat. Add an appropriate amount of Huadiao wine. Blanch for about 1 minute. Skim off the foam, then remove the meat. Rinse thoroughly with warm water to remove surface impurities. Drain well.
**Key point**: Starting with cold water allows blood to slowly release, preventing proteins from solidifying and locking in juices; rinsing with warm water prevents the meat from tightening and becoming tough due to a temperature shock.
4. Add a small amount of oil to the wok. Add the yellow rock sugar. Stir continuously over low heat until the sugar completely melts and turns a fiery red color.
**Key point**: Keep the heat low throughout to prevent the caramel from burning and turning bitter.
5. Add the blanched and drained pork belly to the caramel. Stir-fry for about half a minute, ensuring each piece is evenly coated with the shiny caramel.
**Key point**: Do not stir-fry for too long; just coat evenly.
6. Add the scallion sections, smashed ginger, cinnamon sticks, and star anise. Stir-fry over medium heat until the spices become fragrant.
**Key point**: Identify the star anise correctly to avoid using the toxic *Illicium lanceolatum*.
7. Add an appropriate amount of fermented bean curd sauce. Continue stir-frying to evenly color the meat and absorb the flavor of the bean curd.
**Key point**: The bean curd sauce adds both color and aroma. Stir-fry until evenly incorporated.
8. Add an appropriate amount of sweet bean paste. Stir-fry until the meat pieces are completely coated with the paste, enhancing the savory sauce flavor.
**Key point**: Sweet bean paste gives the dish a rich sauce aroma.
9. Add an appropriate amount of dark soy sauce. Stir-fry until the meat pieces take on a deep, reddish-brown color.
**Key point**: Dark soy sauce is mainly for deepening the color, making the pot meat more visually appealing.
10. Add hot water to the wok. Bring to a boil over high heat.
**Key point**: Always use boiling water to prevent the meat from tightening upon sudden temperature change, which affects tenderness.
11. Add about half a spoon of salt and 1 spoon of sugar to the boiling broth. Stir until completely dissolved.
**Key point**: Use less salt because the fermented bean curd, sweet bean paste, and soy sauce already contain saltiness; sugar enhances flavor and balances the saltiness.
12. Pour the meat and broth from the wok into a glass jar. Add an appropriate amount of cilantro. Cover with a lid. Bring to a boil, then reduce to low heat and simmer for 1 hour.
**Key point**: The cilantro is for aroma; do not add too much. Covering is essential to ensure the meat becomes tender and flavorful.
13. After opening the lid, remove all spices from the broth except the ginger. Then transfer the pot meat into a clay pot for serving.
**Key point**: The ginger can be left in and eaten; well-simmered ginger has a unique flavor. Removing other spices makes the final dish cleaner.

## Health Advice
This pot meat is rich in protein and fat, providing a quick energy boost, making it particularly suitable for winter nourishment or after significant physical exertion. However, pork belly is high in calories, so it is recommended to serve with light vegetables. People with high blood pressure should use less salt and sauces.

# Sourdough Bread
Cuisine: Other
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: All Seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🌾 Bread flour 300g
💧 Water 240g
🍞 Sourdough starter 90g (must be active, with large air pockets, slightly domed top, and increased in volume)
🧂 Salt 6g
🌸 Cranberry powder optional (for color)

## Tools & Equipment
🥣 Glass mixing bowl
⚖️ Digital scale
🍴 Silicone spatula
🫥 Plastic wrap
💨 Spray bottle
🧪 Measuring cup with markings
🔪 Bench scraper
🧺 Banneton (proofing basket)
🧻 Parchment paper
🍳 Dutch oven with lid
🔥 Oven
✂️ Kitchen scissors
🔪 Serrated bread knife
🎒 Airtight bag

## Steps
1. In a bowl, combine bread flour, water, sourdough starter, and salt. Mix with a spatula until no dry flour remains.
   **Key point**: No kneading required, just mix until evenly combined.
2. Rest for 20 minutes. The dough will look loose and rough.
3. Fold and slap the dough several times by hand until it becomes smoother and the roughness disappears. This step is optional and depends on the dough’s condition; the goal is to accelerate gluten development.
4. Rest for another 30 minutes.
5. Perform the first fold: Wet your hands to prevent sticking. Lift the dough from one side of the bowl, stretch it upward, then fold it over to the opposite side. Rotate the bowl and repeat several times until the dough becomes more structured and holds its shape.
6. Rest for 30 minutes.
7. Perform the second fold, repeating the same stretching and folding technique. Wet your hands before starting.
8. Rest for 30 minutes.
9. Perform the third fold: Lightly spray the work surface with water to prevent sticking. Turn out the dough and gently stretch it into a thin rectangle. Roll it up starting from one end. At this point, you can sprinkle in the cranberry powder if desired.
10. Rest for 30 minutes.
11. Perform the fourth fold: Insert both hands under the dough, gently lift the center, and fold the bottom portion up and over. After several folds, the dough should become very smooth.
12. Repeat the coil fold technique three times. The number of folds and the intervals depend on the dough’s condition.
13. First fermentation: Place the folded dough into an oiled measuring cup with markings, and cover with plastic wrap. Ferment until the dough has roughly doubled in volume, using the cup markings to track expansion. Fermentation time varies depending on room temperature and dough condition.
14. Prepare for shaping: On a floured surface, gently stretch the fermented dough into a rectangle. Roll it from top to bottom. With the seam side up, roll it once more along the seam line. Work gently to avoid deflating the dough too much; pop any large air bubbles if needed.
15. Rest for 20 minutes, covering the dough with a large container to prevent drying.
16. Final shaping: Turn the dough over and roll it once more along the seam line. The seam should end up facing up. Pinch the seam and ends firmly to ensure the dough does not open during proofing.
17. Place the shaped dough into a well-floured banneton. Dust the top with more flour as well. Make sure every corner of the banneton is coated with flour to prevent sticking.
18. Cold fermentation: Seal the banneton in two layers of plastic bags and refrigerate overnight. This is the final proofing before baking.
19. Scoring: Turn the cold dough out onto a piece of parchment paper. Use kitchen scissors to cut a pattern into the surface. You can preheat the oven in advance.
20. Baking: Preheat the Dutch oven with its lid in the oven to 500°F (260°C). Using oven mitts, carefully remove the hot Dutch oven, place the dough (on the parchment paper) inside, and cover with the lid.
    - With lid: Bake at 500°F (260°C) for 20 minutes
    - Without lid: Bake at 475°F (245°C) for 20 minutes
    Total baking time is 40 minutes, until the bread is deep golden brown. The covered baking traps steam to help the bread expand, and removing the lid allows the crust to crisp and color.
21. Cooling and slicing: The bread must cool completely before slicing, or the interior will be gummy. If the bottom is difficult to cut, use kitchen scissors to assist.

## Health Notes
This sourdough bread contains probiotics from natural fermentation, which can support gut health. However, bread flour has a relatively high glycemic index, so it pairs well with protein or vegetables. Perfect for those who love a crispy crust and soft crumb; note that cold fermentation enhances the flavor depth.

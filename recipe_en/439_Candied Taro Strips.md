# Candied Taro Strips
Cuisine: Cantonese
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Season: All-year-round

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🍠 Li Pu taro  
🌿 Scallions  

🧂 White granulated sugar or rock sugar  
💧 Water  

## Kitchen Tools & Equipment
🍳 Wok  
🥢 Chopsticks  
🍽️ Plate  

## Steps
1. Cut the Li Pu taro into thick strips.
**Key point**: The taro strips should be of uniform thickness to ensure even cooking during frying.

2. Pour enough cooking oil into the wok and heat to 60% hot.
**Note**: The oil temperature should not be too high to avoid a burnt exterior and raw interior.

3. Add the taro strips and fry until they float and the surface turns slightly golden. They are done when a chopstick can easily pierce through. Remove and drain the oil.
**Key point**: Floating indicates that the internal moisture has evaporated, resulting in a soft and fluffy texture.

4. Pour out and store the oil used for frying the taro.
5. Add white sugar and water into the wok at a ratio of 2:1. Heat over low heat until the sugar is completely dissolved and the syrup forms fine, dense bubbles without changing color.
**Note**: Control the heat to prevent the syrup from caramelizing and turning into a toffee-like state.

6. Sprinkle in the scallions, add the fried taro strips, turn off the heat, and keep stirring continuously. As the temperature drops, the syrup will crystallize to create a candied coating.
**Key point**: Continue stirring even after turning off the heat, using residual heat to evenly coat the taro and allow crystallization.

7. Plate and serve.

## Health Advice
Candied taro strips are high in sugar and oil. It is recommended to control the portion size, especially suitable as a dessert in autumn and winter. Diabetics and those on a weight-loss diet should consume with caution. Pair with a pot of oolong tea to help cut the richness.

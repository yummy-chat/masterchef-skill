# Frosted Hawthorn (Sugar-Coated Hawthorn)
Cuisine: Other
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Autumn (September - October)

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🍎 Fresh hawthorn 500g (must be thoroughly dried, pitted)
💧 Water 100g
🍬 White sugar/soft sugar 200g (rock sugar can also be used; using glucose makes it less prone to dampness, yielding better results)

## Cookware & Equipment
🍳 Wok
🔪 Pitting tool
🥄 Strainer/skimmer
🧻 Kitchen paper towels
⚖️ Digital scale
🌡️ Infrared thermometer (to measure syrup temperature around 80℃)
🥄 Wooden spatula/spatula
💨 Hair dryer (optional, to help accelerate crystallization)
🫙 Vacuum sealer and sealing bags (for storage)

## Preparation Steps
1. Gently rinse the hawthorns with clean water, keeping the fruit intact to avoid the flesh turning red from oxidation upon contact with water. Remove and drain using a strainer, then thoroughly dry the surface moisture with kitchen paper towels.
   **Note**: Hawthorns must be completely dry before pitting, otherwise they are prone to oxidation and discoloration.
2. Pitting: First cut off the calyx at the tail end of the hawthorn. Insert the pitting tool from the stem side, gently press on a cutting board, then pick up the hawthorn and push the core out. Be careful to prevent the flesh from breaking. Do not wash after pitting; set aside directly.
3. Weigh out the ingredients proportionally: 500g hawthorn, 100g water, 200g white sugar.
4. Place the water and white sugar in a cold wok. Turn on the heat and immediately adjust the flame so it does not exceed the surface area of the sugar liquid in the pan. Maintain medium-low heat throughout to prevent the edges from burning.
5. While boiling the syrup, stir gently. When the syrup thickens and can form a continuous thread when lifted with the spatula, immediately turn off the heat. Do not boil until it turns yellow.
6. After turning off the heat, allow the syrup to cool naturally to about 80℃. You can test the temperature by dropping in 1-2 hawthorns first, to avoid the syrup being too hot and cooking the hawthorns, causing the skin to break.
7. Pour the drained hawthorns into the wok with the syrup. Using a wooden spatula, stir quickly, gently, and with broad motions, ensuring each hawthorn is evenly coated with syrup.
8. Continue stirring. The syrup will gradually turn from transparent to white and become grainy. At this point, you can use a hair dryer to blow cool air to accelerate cooling, or let it sit for a while and turn a few more times until an even, snowy white frost forms.
9. Remove the frosted hawthorns and let them cool completely. The finished product is highly prone to absorbing moisture, so it must be stored in an airtight container, or vacuum-sealed and frozen. It will have a crisper texture and a cleaner, sweeter taste.

## Health Advice
This product has a high sugar content. It is recommended to consume no more than 10 pieces per serving. It is suitable for those with poor digestion or loss of appetite, but diabetic patients and those controlling sugar intake should strictly limit consumption. The finished product is highly prone to absorbing moisture and must be stored in an airtight container.

# Maltose
Cuisine: Other
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: All Seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🌾 Wheat grains 150g (soaked for 24 hours, for sprouting)
🍚 Glutinous rice 1000g (soaked for 3 hours, provides starch)
💧 Water as needed (for soaking and steaming)

## Kitchenware & Equipment
🥣 Glass bowl
🍳 Steamer basket and cloth
🌫️ Spray bottle
🔪 Knife and cutting board
🍚 Rice cooker
🩹 Cheesecloth
🍳 Large pot (wok)
🥄 Wooden spatula
🫙 Airtight glass jar

## Steps
1. **Sprout the wheat**
   - Soak wheat grains for 24 hours, drain, and spread them in a single layer on a cloth-lined steamer basket. Do not make the layer too thick.
   - Mist with a spray bottle 3–4 times daily to keep moist, and cover with a cloth to block light.
   - Keep at room temperature until the sprouts grow to 5–6 cm tall.
   **Note**: A layer that is too thick may cause rot and hinder sprouting; blocking light is essential, otherwise the final product will have a grassy flavor; sprout height is a key indicator of enzyme activity.

2. **Prepare the sprouts**
   - Pull up the fully sprouted wheat by the roots, discard any rotten or unsprouted grains, rinse repeatedly in clean water, and drain.
   - Chop on a cutting board, then mince thoroughly to fully release the amylase enzymes.

3. **Steam the glutinous rice**
   - Soak the glutinous rice for 3 hours in advance, wash clean, and add to the rice cooker with slightly more water than usual for cooking rice.
   - Start the cooking program and steam until the rice is very soft and tender.

4. **Mix and ferment**
   - Fluff the steamed rice and let it cool to about 50°C (122°F).
   - Add the minced wheat sprouts, mix thoroughly, then return the mixture to the rice cooker’s inner pot.
   - Press the "Keep Warm" setting and let ferment for 6–8 hours.
   **Note**: If the rice is too hot, it will kill the enzymes; fermenting for too long will make the final product sour.

5. **Filter**
   - Pour the fermented mixture into a container lined with cheesecloth, gather the corners, and squeeze firmly to extract all the maltose liquid. Discard the solids.

6. **Boil the syrup**
   - Pour the liquid into a large pot and bring to a boil over high heat, stirring occasionally to prevent sticking.
   - After boiling, reduce to medium heat and continue boiling. As the water evaporates, the bubbles will change from large to small and dense, and the liquid will darken and thicken.
   - Boil until, when lifted with a wooden spatula, the syrup hangs in a thin sheet from the spatula head, then remove from heat.
   **Note**: Stir continuously throughout, especially toward the end to prevent burning; the doneness indicator is the hanging sheet, not a specific time.

7. **Cool and store**
   - Immediately after removing from heat, stir constantly with a wooden spatula to cool quickly.
   - While the syrup is still liquid enough to pour, transfer it into a clean, airtight glass jar. It will thicken further as it cools naturally.
   **Note**: Do not wait until it is completely cool before jarring, as it will be too thick to pour out; seal and store in a cool, dry place.

## Health Advice
Maltose is rich in carbohydrates and provides quick energy, making it suitable for those with high physical exertion or low blood sugar, but people with diabetes should consume it with caution. It is recommended to eat in small amounts to avoid excessive sugar intake.

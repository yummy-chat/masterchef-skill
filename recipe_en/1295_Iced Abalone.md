# Iced Abalone
Cuisine: Cantonese
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Summer (April-August)

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🐚 Live abalone, about 500g
🍲 Chicken stock, about 1000ml (can substitute with boiling water plus chicken powder or bouillon)
🫚 Ginger, 20g (used in two portions)
🧅 Spring onion, 15g (used in two portions)
💧 Water, 1000ml for blanching, 1000ml for brine
🍊 Xinhui dried tangerine peel, 3g (needs to be soaked in advance)
🌿 Star anise, 3g
🌿 Cinnamon stick, 3g
🌿 Bay leaves, 2g
🌿 Sichuan peppercorns, 3g
🌿 Black cardamom, 3g
🌿 Kaempferia galanga, 3g
🌿 Licorice root, 3g
🌿 Angelica sinensis, 3g
🧂 Salt, to taste (use a generous amount for cleaning, can be on the heavier side for brine seasoning)
🍬 Yellow rock sugar, to taste (for a hint of sweetness in the brine)
🍶 Huadiao wine, 200ml (per liter of broth base, add after cooling)
🍾 Rose dew, 50ml (per liter of broth base, add after cooling)
🐟 Fish sauce, to taste (add after cooling, for umami enhancement)

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment
🌡️ Digital thermometer
🔫 Kitchen torch
🥣 Glass bowl or basin
🍲 Clay pot
🪥 Soft-bristled toothbrush (children's size)
🍱 Airtight glass container

## Preparation Steps
1.  Place the live abalones in a large bowl. Add a generous amount of salt and mix quickly and evenly. Pour in 50℃ warm water. Gently rub the abalones with your fingers to remove most of the black mucus from the surface.
**Key Point**: The salt causes the abalone to contract, firm up, and secrete mucus; warm water aids in cleaning.
2.  Turn the abalone upside down. Use a kitchen torch to sear the shell side, then immediately plunge it into cold water to cool. Gently remove the shell with your fingers. Discard the internal organs and the mouth. Rub with salt again and rinse thoroughly under clean water.
**Key Point**: The combination of torch searing and cold shock is the key technique for removing the shell.
3.  Use the children's soft-bristled toothbrush to carefully scrub the black frill and the flesh surface of the abalone until it is clean and white.
**Note**: Do not use an adult's hard-bristled toothbrush to avoid damaging the smooth surface of the abalone.
4.  Add water, ginger slices, and spring onion segments to a pot. Heat to around 90℃. Place the cleaned abalones in the water, ensuring the temperature does not boil. Gently poach for 8 minutes.
**Key Point**: Low-temperature slow poaching preserves the abalone's tenderness and prevents it from becoming tough and overcooked.
5.  Immediately remove the poached abalones and plunge them into ice water. Allow them to soak so that, through the process of thermal expansion and contraction, their texture becomes exceptionally crisp.
6.  Prepare the chicken stock in a clay pot. Add ginger slices, a tied spring onion knot, all the dried spices and herbs, and the water used to soak the tangerine peel. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to low heat and simmer for 30 minutes to allow the flavors to fully meld.
7.  Turn off the heat. Season the brine with salt and yellow rock sugar.
**Note**: Since the dish is served cold, which can dull flavors, the seasoning can be on the heavier side.
8.  Strain all the spice solids from the cooked brine. Let the brine cool completely, then add the Huadiao wine, rose dew, and fish sauce according to the proportions. Stir well to combine.
**Key Point**: The wine must be added to the cooled brine to preserve its aroma.
9.  Submerge all the prepared abalones completely in the prepared soaking liquid. Seal the container and refrigerate for 24 hours to marinate. It is then ready to serve.

## Health Advice
Abalone prepared through low-temperature slow poaching and iced chilling has a crisp texture. It is rich in protein and low in fat, making it suitable for summer nutrition. However, the brine contains a considerable amount of seasonings; individuals with high blood pressure are advised to consume it in small quantities.

# Japanese Tonkotsu Ramen 🍜
Cuisine: Other
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: All Seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🦴 Pork backbone, approx. 1500g (ask the butcher to chop into pieces)
🦴 Pork leg bones, 2 pieces
🐷 Pork trotter, 1 piece
🌾 Bread flour, 400g
🥚 Whole egg liquid, 25g
🧂 Alkaline water (kansui), 4g (must be fully dissolved with egg liquid and water)
💧 Water (for dough), 110g
🧂 Salt (for dough), 4g
🧅 Onion, half
🥕 Carrot, a small piece
🧄 Garlic, 2 cloves
🧅 Scallion leaves, 2 pieces
🍄 Shiitake mushrooms, 2 pieces
🥬 Cabbage, approx. 2g
🥩 Pork belly (or pork shoulder), as needed (for chashu)
🥚 Eggs, 5 pieces (for soft-boiled eggs)
🍶 Soy sauce, 200ml (for chashu sauce) + 100ml (for soft-boiled egg sauce)
🍶 Mirin, 100ml (for chashu) + 100ml (for soft-boiled eggs)
🍶 Sake, 100ml (for chashu) + 50ml (for soft-boiled eggs)
💧 Water, 100ml (for soft-boiled egg sauce)
🍬 Sugar, 10g
🫚 Ginger, grated, to taste
🧄 Garlic, 1 clove (sliced)
🍶 Seasoned soy sauce (for bowl base), 30g (containing dried scallops, kelp, etc., for assembly)

## Kitchen Tools & Equipment
🔥 Blowtorch (for treating trotter & searing chashu)
🪣 Deep stock pot
🍝 Pasta machine
🌡️ Sous vide immersion circulator
📦 Vacuum sealer
🛍️ Resealable bags
🔥 Oven
🫗 Strainer
⚖️ Kitchen scale
🥢 Noodle strainer (long chopsticks/slotted spoon)
🔪 Knife

## Preparation Steps
1.  Have the pork backbone and leg bones chopped; treat the pork trotter by scorching with a blowtorch or blanching in boiling water to remove any gamey odor. Soak all bones in cold water for 1.5 to 2 hours, then discard the bloodied water. Place in a pot of cold water, blanch, and skim off any scum. Remove and rinse the bones clean. In a separate pot, add fresh water and the bones, bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to medium-high heat. Continuously skim off any foam and maintain a rolling boil for approx. 3 hours, until the broth is reduced to 1/3 of its original volume.
   **Key Point**: Blanching with cold water removes impurities more thoroughly; maintain a constant medium-high boil to develop an opaque, milky white broth.
2.  Strain the first batch of broth and set aside. Crush the bones in the pot with a rolling pin. Add enough fresh water, skim off any foam, and again maintain a medium-high rolling boil for approx. 3 hours.
   **Key Point**: Crushing the bones releases more flavor; the second batch of broth will be whiter and thicker.
3.  Repeat the process, adding water and boiling for approx. 1 hour. Remove the leg bones, pick out the marrow, and return it to the pot. Crush any small bones and add them back. Continue to boil until the liquid is reduced to 1/3. Pour all the strained broth from the first two batches back into the pot. Add the onion, carrot, garlic, scallion leaves, shiitake mushrooms, and cabbage. Simmer for another 2 hours, adding water as needed to maintain the liquid level. Total broth simmering time is approx. 13 hours.
   **Key Point**: Bone marrow is the essence of flavor and must be added; vegetables neutralize any gaminess and add sweetness.
4.  Combine the whole egg liquid, water, and alkaline water until the alkali is fully dissolved. Mix the bread flour and salt. Gradually add the liquid mixture, stirring until it forms shaggy crumbs. Place the crumbs in a resealable bag and rest for 30 minutes. Press or step on the bag to compress the crumbs into a cohesive sheet. Run the sheet through the pasta machine repeatedly until smooth, then cut into noodles approx. 1.5mm wide. Portion into approx. 100g per serving, package in resealable bags, and rest in the refrigerator for 24 hours to mature.
   **Key Point**: Alkaline water must be fully dissolved; thorough kneading develops gluten; resting in the fridge improves texture.
5.  Roll the pork belly into a tight cylinder and tie it with kitchen twine. Roast in an oven preheated to 275°C for approx. 20 minutes until the surface is browned and crispy and fat has rendered. Remove and combine with ginger slices, garlic cloves, soy sauce, mirin, and sake in a vacuum bag. Seal and cook sous vide at 70°C for approx. 3 hours. Once done, refrigerate to set.
   **Key Point**: High-heat roasting renders fat and enhances flavor; sous vide keeps the meat tender and juicy.
6.  Place the eggs in cold water and bring to a boil. Once boiling, reduce to low heat and repeatedly “lift and lower” the eggs to ensure even cooking. Boil until the yolks are jammy. Transfer to an ice bath to cool, then gently peel the shells. Place the eggs in a vacuum bag with a mixture of soy sauce, mirin, sake, water, sugar, grated ginger, and sliced garlic. Seal and marinate in the refrigerator overnight.
   **Key Point**: The “lift and lower” method prevents cracking; overnight marinating ensures deep flavor.
7.  Add 30g of seasoned soy sauce base to each serving bowl. Heat the tonkotsu broth until it is at a rolling boil, then ladle 300ml into each bowl. In a separate pot of boiling water, cook one serving of the matured alkaline noodles for approx. 30 seconds. Quickly drain and place them in the soup bowl, giving them a slight lift and stir. Top with sliced chashu and a halved soft-boiled egg. Garnish with chopped scallions.
   **Key Point**: The broth must be vigorously boiling; the noodles cook for a very short time to retain their firm, chewy texture.

## Health Advice
This tonkotsu ramen has a rich, concentrated broth and firm noodles, but the total simmering time is quite long. It is recommended to consume in moderation to avoid a high-fat, high-sodium load. It is suitable as an occasional indulgence for fitness enthusiasts. Be mindful of the portions of soft-boiled egg and chashu to prevent excessive sodium intake.

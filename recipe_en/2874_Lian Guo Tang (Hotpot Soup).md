# Lian Guo Tang (Hotpot Soup)
Cuisine: Sichuan
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Winter (November-December)

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🥕 White radish 1 (peeled twice, cut into slices about the thickness of two coins)
🥩 Pork belly 1 piece (about the size of a palm, blanched in advance until just cooked)
🫚 Ginger 2 slices (for removing pork smell during cooking)
🧅 Scallions 2 stalks (whole, for removing pork smell during cooking)
🍶 Yellow wine (Shaoxing wine) appropriate amount (for removing pork smell)
🫒 Cooking oil appropriate amount (for stir-frying soup base and dipping sauce)
⭐ Star anise 1 piece
🌿 Sichuan peppercorns a few
🫚 Ginger 3-4 slices (for stir-frying soup base)
🧅 Scallion cut into sections (for stir-frying soup base)
🫘 Pixian broad bean paste 2 parts
🫘 Lao Gan Ma chili sauce 1 part
🧄 Minced ginger appropriate amount (for dipping sauce)
🧄 Minced garlic appropriate amount (for dipping sauce)
🍶 Cooking wine appropriate amount (for dipping sauce)
💧 Water appropriate amount (for dipping sauce)
🍬 Sugar appropriate amount (for seasoning dipping sauce)
🧂 Light soy sauce appropriate amount (for seasoning dipping sauce)
🧂 White pepper powder appropriate amount (for seasoning dipping sauce)
🥜 Crushed peanuts appropriate amount (for enhancing aroma in dipping sauce; can substitute with crushed soybeans or white sesame seeds)
🧂 Salt about 3 grams (for seasoning soup)

## Kitchenware & Equipment
🍳 Wok or frying pan
🥄 Soup ladle
🔪 Chef's knife, cutting board
🥒 Vegetable peeler
🥣 Glass bowl

## Cooking Steps
1. Use a vegetable peeler to remove the outer skin and inner pungent layer of the white radish. Cut into slices about the thickness of two coins and soak in clean water until ready to use.
**Note**: Both layers of skin must be removed, otherwise the radish will taste pungent.

2. Place the pork belly in cold water in a pot. Add ginger slices, whole scallions, and a little yellow wine. Cook over low heat until just cooked through, then remove. Reserve the pork broth for later use. Once the meat has cooled, cut it against the grain into thin slices.
3. Add a little cooking oil to the wok. Add star anise, Sichuan peppercorns, ginger slices, and scallion sections. Sauté over low heat until fragrant. Add the drained radish slices and stir-fry until the edges become slightly translucent.
4. Pour in the reserved pork broth. Bring to a boil over high heat, then add the sliced pork belly. Reduce to medium heat and simmer until the radish is completely translucent. Keep the wok uncovered throughout cooking to prevent any radish odor from lingering in the soup.
**Key step**: Do not cover the wok at any time, otherwise the radish's odor will be trapped in the soup.

5. In a separate pan, add a little cooking oil. Over low heat, add Pixian broad bean paste and stir-fry until the red oil releases. Then add Lao Gan Ma chili sauce, minced ginger, and minced garlic. Stir-fry until fragrant. Splash in cooking wine and stir well.
6. Add an appropriate amount of water and bring to a boil. Season with sugar, light soy sauce, and white pepper powder. Simmer over low heat for a moment to allow the flavors to meld. Just before turning off the heat, stir in the crushed peanuts. Transfer to a bowl and garnish with chopped scallions to make the dipping sauce.
7. Before serving the Lian Guo Tang, season with about 3 grams of salt, stir well, and turn off the heat. Ladle into bowls. Garnish with cilantro if desired.

## Health Tips
The combination of white radish and pork belly warms the body in winter and is easy to digest, making it suitable for people who tend to feel cold. Remember to blanch the pork belly to remove any gamey smell. Keep the dipping sauce low in oil and salt for a healthier option.

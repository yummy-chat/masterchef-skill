# Soft Noodle Liang Pi (Cold Skin Noodles)  
Cuisine: Shaanxi Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Summer, April to August  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🌾 High-gluten flour 500g (or all-purpose flour, add salt to strengthen)  
💧 Water 850ml (add in two portions: first 450ml, then 400ml)  
🧂 Salt 4g  
🥒 Cucumber, appropriate amount (shredded)  
🌱 Bean sprouts, appropriate amount (blanched)  
🍥 Gluten, appropriate amount (steamed from washed dough, then cut into pieces)  
🫘 Pickled long beans, appropriate amount  
🥜 Crushed peanuts, appropriate amount  
🧅 Chopped green onions, appropriate amount  
🌶️ Fresh or dried chili peppers, appropriate amount (optional)  
🫗 Cooking oil, a small amount (brush on pan to prevent sticking, and on liang pi surface to prevent sticking)  
🌶️ Chili oil, appropriate amount (can substitute with minced garlic chili sauce or Laoganma)  

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment  
🥣 Glass bowl (or mixing basin)  
🔧 Silicone spatula/scraper  
🪣 Stainless steel strainer  
🍳 Stainless steel flat pan (liang pi mold)  
🖌️ Oil brush  
🍲 Steamer  

## Steps  
1. Mix 500g high-gluten flour with 4g salt, add 450ml water, stir until no dry flour remains and a dough forms, no kneading needed.  
2. Cover with plastic wrap and rest for 30 minutes. During this time, lift the dough with a spatula and slap it against the bowl more than ten times to promote gluten network formation.  
3. Add the remaining 400ml water to the rested dough, stir continuously with a spatula until the starch is fully dissolved into the water, forming a white starch slurry.  
4. Strain the mixture through a strainer. Rinse the gluten caught in the strainer with clean water, then steam until cooked and cut into pieces for later use. The strained starch water is the liang pi slurry.  
5. Brush a thin layer of oil on the bottom of the stainless steel flat pan. Stir the starch slurry well, then ladle a spoonful into the pan and shake to spread the liquid evenly across the bottom.  
6. Once the steamer water is boiling, place the flat pan inside, cover, and steam over medium-high heat for 2–3 minutes, until large bubbles rise on the liang pi surface, indicating it is cooked.  
7. Remove the steamed pan and immerse it in cold water to cool. Brush a layer of cooking oil on the liang pi surface, then use both hands to peel the liang pi off completely from the edge.  
8. Evenly brush a layer of chili oil on the liang pi. Top with steamed gluten pieces, cucumber shreds, bean sprouts, pickled long beans, crushed peanuts, and chopped green onions as desired.  
9. Fold both sides of the liang pi inward, then roll tightly from bottom to top for serving. The toppings can be adjusted according to preference.  

## Health Advice  
Liang pi is primarily made from flour, with relatively low calories but limited protein content. It is recommended to pair it with gluten and bean sprouts for increased satiety. Cucumber shreds and pickled long beans provide vitamins, but chili oil should be used in moderation. Suitable for cooling off in summer, but those with sensitive stomachs should reduce spiciness.

# Salt and Pepper Mantis Shrimp  
Cuisine: Cantonese  
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: All-year-round

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🦐 Mantis shrimp 6 pieces, trim off the tail spines and side legs  
🌽 Cornstarch (for coating the shrimp)  
🫚 Ginger approx. 1 tbsp, finely minced  
🧄 Garlic approx. 1 tbsp, finely minced  
🧅 Shallot approx. 1 tbsp, minced  
🌶️ Chili peppers (green and red) approx. ½ each, sliced into rings  
🍶 Cooking wine (for wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🍾 Light soy sauce a small amount (for wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🧂 Sichuan peppercorn powder to taste (for wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🧂 Five-spice powder to taste (for wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🧂 White pepper powder to taste (for wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🥫 Doubanjiang (broad bean paste) to taste (for wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🍬 Sugar a small amount (to enhance flavor, for wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🫒 Sesame oil a small amount (for wet salt and pepper sauce, added last)  
🍳 Cooking oil sufficient amount (for deep frying)  
🍷 Liquor to taste (drizzled along the wok edge during stir-frying, then ignited to enhance wok hei)

## Cookware and Equipment  
🍳 Wok  
🥄 Slotted spoon / Strainer  
🥄 Ladle  
🥣 Small bowl (for mixing the wet salt and pepper sauce)  
🍽️ Plate (for holding the aromatics and dry powder)

## Cooking Steps  
1. Trim off the tail spines and side legs of the mantis shrimp to avoid injuring the mouth while eating.  
2. Prepare the wet salt and pepper sauce: In a small bowl, sequentially add cooking wine, a small amount of light soy sauce, Sichuan peppercorn powder, five-spice powder, white pepper powder, and Doubanjiang. Stir to combine. Add a small amount of sugar and finally add sesame oil, mixing well.  
**Note**: Mantis shrimp naturally carry a salty taste from the sea, so no additional salt is added to the sauce. The wet mixture adheres more easily to the shrimp than dry salt and pepper.

3. Finely mince the ginger, garlic, and shallot to the size of rice grains. Slice the green and red chili peppers into rings. Set aside.  
4. Evenly dust the surface of the prepared and drained shrimp with a thin layer of cornstarch. Turn the shrimp over and dust the other side, ensuring both sides are coated.  
**Key point**: Cornstarch locks in the shrimp juices, making the meat more tender.

5. Heat oil in a wok to approximately 150°C. Deep-fry the mantis shrimp for about 2 minutes, until the shells turn red and set. Remove from the oil.  
6. Increase the oil temperature to 160-170°C. Deep-fry the shrimp a second time for about 1 minute, until the shells are golden and crispy. Remove and drain the oil.  
**Key point**: The second frying makes the shells drier and crispier.

7. Leave a small amount of oil in the wok. Over low heat, sauté the minced ginger, garlic, and shallot until fragrant. Add the chili pepper rings and stir-fry briefly.  
8. Pour in the prepared wet salt and pepper sauce. Bring to a boil, then add the fried shrimp. Reduce the heat to low.  
9. Drizzle a splash of liquor along the edge of the wok. As the flame ignites, quickly toss the wok to stir-fry evenly, ensuring the sauce coats the shrimp thoroughly. Remove from the wok.  
**Note**: Adding liquor and igniting it is a technique to create “wok hei.” The action must be quick, and safety should be observed.

## Health Advice  
This salt and pepper mantis shrimp dish is rich in protein and low in fat, making it suitable for muscle building. However, due to the deep-frying and the high sodium content of Doubanjiang, those with high blood pressure, high blood sugar, or high cholesterol should consume it in small amounts. The fried shells can be eaten whole for a calcium boost, but be cautious of the risk of pricking the mouth.

# Huangtian Taro-Braised Pork Belly 🥘
Cuisine: Other
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings 🛒
🐖 1 piece of skin-on pork belly (about 3000g)
🍠 3 large taro roots (recommended from Guangxi for a fluffy, starchy texture)
🫚 1 large piece of ginger (smashed)
🧅 A few scallions (tied into knots)
🍃 Green tea leaves, as needed
🍄 Small dried shiitake mushrooms, as needed (rinsed clean)
🍒 1 red date per bowl (halved and pitted)
🍯 Honey, as needed
🧂 Salt, as needed
🫘 5 portions of fermented bean curd (including sauce)
🍶 30g high-proof rice wine (52% ABV)
🥄 150g light soy sauce
🥄 30g dark soy sauce
🌿 About 1g freshly ground star anise powder
🌿 About 1g freshly ground cinnamon powder
🍬 300g rock sugar (for making syrup)

## Tools & Equipment 🛠️
🔪 Cutting board, chef's knife
🥘 Stockpot
🍳 Wok
🥄 Slotted spoon
🥣 Metal bowl (for mixing, layering)
🔨 Specialized pork skin pricker
♨️ Steamer
🍽️ Serving plate (for flipping)

## Instructions 📝
1. Scrape off any remaining hair from the pork belly, then rinse thoroughly under clean water.
2. Fill a stockpot with water, add pork belly, smashed ginger, scallion knots, and a little honey. Bring to a boil over high heat, skim off any foam. Add green tea leaves, reduce heat to low, and simmer for 30 minutes.
**Note**: Honey helps the skin caramelize; tea leaves remove gaminess and reduce greasiness.
3. Remove pork belly and pat dry with paper towels. Evenly prick the skin all over using a specialized pork skin pricker. Pat dry again, then lightly rub salt over the skin.
**Key**: Pricking deeply allows fat to render and deepens flavor; salt prevents oil splattering during frying.
4. Heat the wok over high heat, add a generous amount of oil, and heat to 350°F (180°C). Place pork skin-side down into the oil, immediately covering with a lid for protection. Fry until the skin turns golden brown, then remove. Immediately submerge in the reserved poaching liquid for 20 minutes.
**Key**: Use caution to avoid burns; soaking plumps the skin while tea further removes greasiness.
5. Peel and rinse the taro, trim into rectangular blocks, slice into thick pieces matching the pork belly slices, and set aside scrap pieces.
6. Deep-fry both taro slices and scrap pieces in the oil until fully cooked.
**Note**: Do not stir taro immediately after adding; wait until it firms up before gently moving to prevent breakage.
7. Prepare the seasoning sauce: In a bowl, crush the fermented bean curd and sauce into a paste. Gradually add rice wine, light soy sauce, dark soy sauce, star anise powder, and cinnamon powder, stirring until well combined.
8. Make the syrup: In a pot, combine rock sugar with twice the amount of water. Cook over low heat until the syrup thickens slightly, then remove and let cool to room temperature.
9. Remove soaked pork belly and slice into 1cm-thick connected pieces (matching taro slice size). Place pork slices into seasoning sauce and cooled syrup, toss to coat evenly, and marinate for 10 minutes.
10. Insert one taro slice into the cut seam of each pork slice. Arrange these pork-taro bundles skin-side down neatly in a bowl.
11. Top the arranged bundles with a few dried shiitake mushrooms and one halved red date per bowl.
12. Layer fried taro scrap pieces on top, then pour any remaining marinade evenly over everything.
13. Bring steamer water to a boil, place the bowl(s) inside, reduce to medium-low heat, and steam for 2 hours.
14. Once steamed, remove the bowl and carefully pour off the accumulated liquid. Place a serving plate over the bowl, then quickly invert to flip the pork and taro onto the plate. Finally, drizzle the reserved liquid back over the top before serving.

## Health Note 🥦
This dish is high in fat and sugar. Pair with sautéed seasonal vegetables and multigrain rice to balance nutrition, and enjoy in moderation during gatherings. While pricking the skin and deep-frying the taro add extra steps, they significantly reduce greasiness.

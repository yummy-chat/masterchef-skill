# Braised Pork Hock with Aged Vinegar  
Cuisine: Northeast Chinese  
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: All seasons  

## Ingredients & Seasonings  
🍖 1 pork hock (front hock is leaner, rear hock is fattier; fresh cold-cut meat recommended)  
🧅 Scallion (适量)  
🧄 Ginger (适量)  
🌿 Cinnamon stick (适量)  
⭐ Star anise (适量)  
🍶 3 tbsp yellow wine  
🍬 1 tbsp caramel color (can substitute with half tbsp rock sugar)  
🥄 2 tbsp light soy sauce  
🥄 Slightly more than half tbsp dark soy sauce  
🫗 About 1 tbsp aged vinegar (black vinegar; if the hock is very fatty, use 2–3 tbsp)  
🧂 About 10g salt  
🍬 About half tbsp rock sugar  
🌽 Cornstarch (适量, for thickening)  

## Cookware & Equipment  
♨️ Blowtorch (optional, for burning off pig skin to remove odor)  
🪣 Stainless steel basin  
🍳 Wok or cast iron pot  
⚡ Electric pressure cooker  
🥄 Stainless steel ladle  
🥣 Cornstarch bowl  
🪥 Strainer  
🔧 Tongs (tweezers)  

## Instructions  

### Preparation Stage  
1. **Wash the pork hock**: Rinse the pork hock under cold water.  
2. **Cold water blanching**: Place the pork hock in a pot with cold water—the water must completely submerge the hock. Once it comes to a boil, reduce to medium-low heat and cook for at least half an hour. Turn occasionally to prevent sticking.  
**Key point**: This long boiling step removes the raw meaty smell.  
3. **Remove residual hair**: Rinse the blanched hock under cold water until cool enough to handle, then use tweezers to carefully pluck out any remaining fine hairs.  

### Cooking Stage  
1. **Season in the pot**: Place the prepared pork hock into the inner pot of the electric pressure cooker. Add scallion, ginger, cinnamon stick, star anise, then add yellow wine, caramel color, light soy sauce, dark soy sauce, aged vinegar, and salt in the given proportions.  
2. **Add boiling water and braise**: You must add boiling water—the water level should always cover the skin of the hock throughout cooking, to prevent the skin from hardening due to dehydration. Set the electric pressure cooker to 40 kPa and pressure-cook for about 90 minutes, then allow natural pressure release.  
**Doneness check**: The hock is done when a chopstick can easily pierce through the meat—it should be tender and falling off the bone.  
3. **Remove the hock and reduce the braising liquid**: Take out the braised hock, and strain out the aromatics from the liquid. Pour the liquid into a wok and boil it down over high heat. Taste and adjust with salt, rock sugar, or aged vinegar as needed, ensuring the liquid is well-balanced in saltiness with a distinct vinegar fragrance.  
4. **Thicken the sauce**: When the liquid has reduced to about three ladlefuls, slowly add the settled cornstarch-water mixture in several additions, waiting for the liquid to come back to a boil each time. Continue until the sauce is thick enough to flow from the ladle in a steady stream.  
5. **Serve with sauce**: Pour the thickened sauce directly over the plated pork hock.  

## Health Notes  
Pork hock is rich in collagen, making it suitable for those needing protein supplementation or joint care. However, it is high in calories, and people with gout should eat it sparingly. Adding aged vinegar during braising helps cut through the richness and aids digestion. Pair with vegetables for balanced nutrition.

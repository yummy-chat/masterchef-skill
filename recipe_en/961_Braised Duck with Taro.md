# Braised Duck with Taro
Cuisine: Cantonese  
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Season: Suitable for all year round  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🦆 1 Muscovy duck (firm flesh, thin skin, and brittle bones)  
🥔 About one small plate of small taro (pre-steamed, peeled, and halved)  
🫚 A few slices of ginger  
🧄 A few cloves of garlic (smashed or halved)  
🍊 1 small piece of dried tangerine peel (5-year aged, torn into pieces)  
🍬 1 small piece of slab sugar  
🌿 1 small handful of perilla leaves  
🧅 A few stalks of scallion (cut into sections)  

🫘 About 1 tablespoon of chu hou paste  
🥜 About 1 tablespoon of peanut butter  
🦪 About 1 tablespoon of oyster sauce  
🧂 About 1 tablespoon of light soy sauce  
🧂 A dash of dark soy sauce  
🥜 Appropriate amount of peanut oil  
💧 Appropriate amount of water (or stock), enough to submerge the duck  

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment  
🍳 Wok  
🔥 Blowtorch (to singe off fine feathers and remove gamey smell from duck skin)  
✂️ Scissors (to trim the duck tail)  
♨️ Steamer (to steam the taro)  
🥘 Clay pot (for final serving and heat retention)  

## Cooking Steps  
### Prepare Ingredients  
1. Wash the duck thoroughly. Use a blowtorch to singe any remaining fine feathers on the skin until the skin turns golden, eliminating any feathery odor. Trim off the duck tail.
2. Place the whole small taro in a steamer and steam for about 15 minutes until cooked. Remove, peel off the skin, and cut each into halves.
3. Chop the duck into small pieces. Slice the ginger, smash the garlic, and cut the scallion into sections.
### Searing the Duck Pieces  
4. Place the chopped duck pieces into a dry wok (no oil). Stir-fry over medium heat until moisture and fat are rendered out and the surface of the duck changes color. Remove and set aside.  
**Key point**: Dry-searing without oil helps draw out excess moisture and fat, removing gamey smell and preserving freshness.  

### Aromatics and Seasoning  
5. Heat the wok, add peanut oil, and stir-fry the ginger slices and garlic cloves until they turn golden brown.
6. Return the duck pieces to the wok and stir-fry to combine. Add the chu hou paste and peanut butter, and stir-fry quickly until the sauces are evenly coated on the duck and fragrant.  
**Note**: Stir-fry the duck first before adding the sauces.  

### Braising  
7. Add enough water to submerge the duck. Add the torn dried tangerine peel and slab sugar, then pour in the oyster sauce, light soy sauce, and a dash of dark soy sauce. Stir to combine.
8. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to medium heat, cover, and braise for about 25 minutes.  
### Adding the Taro  
9. Remove the lid, add the steamed taro pieces. Gently turn them to immerse in the sauce, cover again, and continue braising over medium heat for about 15 minutes.  
### Reducing the Sauce and Enhancing Aroma  
10. Remove the lid and check the sauce. If a thicker consistency is desired, increase the heat to high to reduce slightly. Just before turning off the heat, scatter in the scallion sections and perilla leaves, and quickly stir-fry a few times.  
**Key point**: Add perilla just before finishing to preserve its unique aroma.  

11. Transfer the dish to a preheated clay pot to keep warm before serving.  

## Health Advice  
This dish offers a balanced combination of protein and carbohydrates. Duck meat has a cooling nature, making it suitable for moistening dryness in autumn and winter. Taro is rich in dietary fiber, aiding digestion. The duck skin is high in fat; those with high blood lipids are advised to remove the skin before eating.

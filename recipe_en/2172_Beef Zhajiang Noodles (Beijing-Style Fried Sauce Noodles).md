# Beef Zhajiang Noodles (Beijing-Style Fried Sauce Noodles)
Cuisine: Beijing Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🥩 Beef chuck (about 500g, trim off connective tissue, cut into 1.5cm thick slices)  
🍜 Noodles (to taste)  
🫘 Dried yellow soybean paste (1 pack, about 150-200g, dilute with a little water in advance)  
🧅 Scallions (to taste, half cut into scallion pieces for stir-frying, half cut into scallion pieces for finishing)  
🧄 Garlic (to taste, sliced)  
🫚 Ginger (to taste, sliced)  
🌱 Mung bean sprouts (to taste, blanched)  
🥕 Water radish (to taste, shredded)  
🥬 Celery (to taste, diced)  
🥒 Cucumber (to taste, cut into strips)  
🫘 Soybeans (to taste, pre-cooked)  
⭐ Star anise (2 pieces)  
🫑 Sichuan peppercorns (about 20 pieces)  
🫒 Sesame oil (to taste)  
🍬 White sugar (about 3 small spoons)  
🧂 White pepper powder (about 2g)

## Kitchen Utensils & Equipment
🍳 Wok  
🪵 Wooden cutting board  
🔪 Chef’s knife  
🥄 Slotted spoon  
🥣 Large bowl  
♨️ Stove

## Cooking Steps
1. Trim the beef chuck of connective tissue and cut into slices about 1.5cm thick.  
**Key point**: Choosing chuck yields tender meat; removing connective tissue prevents it from getting stuck between teeth.

2. Slice the ginger, slice the garlic, and cut the scallions into pieces. Pre-cook the soybeans, blanch the mung bean sprouts, shred the water radish, dice the celery, and cut the cucumber into strips; set aside.
3. Place the entire pack of dried yellow soybean paste into a bowl, add a small amount of water, and mix well by hand to dilute. Since it will be simmered for a long time, dilute the paste a bit more to prevent it from sticking to the pan too early.
4. Heat a generous amount of oil in the wok over medium heat. Add the beef slices and stir-fry until the moisture evaporates, the surface of the meat shrinks, oil renders out, and the color darkens. Remove and set aside.
5. Leave a thin layer of oil in the wok, reduce to low heat, add the star anise and Sichuan peppercorns, and fry until fragrant and the spices slightly change color. Remove them with a slotted spoon, retaining the flavored oil.
6. Maintain low heat, first add the garlic slices, ginger slices, and most of the scallion pieces, stir-fry until fragrant, then pour in the diluted yellow soybean paste. Keep the heat low and stir continuously with a spatula. Slowly simmer until the sauce starts to bubble vigorously, much of the water evaporates, and oil separates from the paste. At this point, the sauce will darken and thicken, and the raw paste flavor will turn into a rich aroma. This process is called "糗酱" (simmering the paste), requiring patience to prevent burning on the bottom.
7. When the sauce is thick and oil is visible, add the white pepper powder and white sugar, and stir well. The sugar amount is about 3 small spoons, used to enhance freshness and balance the saltiness of the paste; white pepper adds a mildly spicy aroma.
8. Return the stir-fried beef slices to the sauce pot and stir evenly. Just before turning off the heat, sprinkle in the remaining raw scallion pieces, drizzle in an appropriate amount of sesame oil, mix well, and remove from heat.
9. In a separate pot, cook the noodles until done. Drain and optionally rinse with cold water, then drain again and place into bowls. Ladle the beef zhajiang sauce over the noodles, then top with cucumber strips, bean sprouts, soybeans, water radish shreds, celery dices, and other toppings. Mix well and serve. The finished dish has a rich, savory sauce with a touch of sweetness; the meat slices are tender yet chewy, and the crisp toppings add layered textures.

## Health Advice
Using beef chuck for the zhajiang sauce provides rich protein, making it suitable for muscle building. However, the yellow soybean paste is salty, so be mindful of salt intake. The noodles portion can be halved, and pairing with plenty of vegetable toppings offers more balanced fiber.

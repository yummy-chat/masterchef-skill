# Taro Milk Ice
Cuisine: Jiangsu Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Summer (April–August)

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🍠 1/4 Lipu taro (sliced and steamed)  
🍠 1 small purple sweet potato (sliced and steamed, for color)  
🥡 Approximately 1/5 bag of cassava starch (adjust based on dough dryness)  
🥛 1 box of milk (about 200–250ml)  
🍦 2 small pudding popsicles (in place of traditional shaved ice)  
🫘 Appropriate amount of honey red beans (or other preferred fillings)  
🍯 Appropriate amount of condensed milk (adds sweetness and creaminess to the taro paste)  
🍬 Appropriate amount of sugar (mixed into cooked taro balls to prevent sticking)

## Kitchenware & Equipment
♨️ Dormitory self-heating pot (includes inner pot and heating pack)  
🍶 Insulated cup (holds boiling water for scalding starch)  
🎒 Ziplock bag (for hand-mashing taro paste)  
🔪 Cutting board  
🍴 Fork/spoon  
🥣 Bowl/cup

## Steps
1. Slice the Lipu taro and purple sweet potato, then arrange them in the inner pot of the self-heating pot.  
2. Place a 90g heating pack in the bottom layer of the self-heating pot, add water, set the inner pot inside, close the lid, and open the vent. Heat for 20 to 30 minutes until ingredients are fully cooked.  
**Note**: The vent must be open to utilize steam heating.  
3. Remove some steamed taro and purple sweet potato, then add cassaba starch while still hot.  
4. Gradually pour freshly boiled water over the starch in small amounts, stirring continuously. Adjust water or dry starch based on dryness.  
**Note**: If the dough falls apart, blanch a small piece in boiling water for 3 seconds, then remove and knead with dry starch to fix.  
5. Knead into a smooth, pliable dough. The dough should be elastic and not crack easily when pressed.  
6. Roll the dough into long ropes, cut into small pieces, and dust with cassava starch to prevent sticking. This forms the raw taro balls.  
7. Place the remaining steamed taro and purple sweet potato into a ziplock bag, add condensed milk, and mash by hand.  
**Texture choice**: Hand-mashing retains a slightly coarse texture; you can also use a machine for a fine, smooth paste.  
8. When water boils, drop in the taro balls. Cook until they all float to the surface, then boil for an additional 2 minutes.  
**Key point**: Floating is an important sign of doneness. Ensure the cassava starch is fully cooked.  
9. Remove the cooked taro balls, rinse in cold water, drain, then toss with sugar to prevent sticking.  
10. Layer the bottom of a bowl with taro paste, then add taro balls, pudding popsicles, and other fillings. Finally, pour in milk.  
**Variation**: Mix leftover taro paste with milk to make taro milk.

## Health Advice
Taro paste and cassava starch provide carbohydrates and a small amount of dietary fiber. This dish is suitable as a cooling summer snack, not as a meal replacement. When using a self-heating pot, be cautious of steam burns. Ensure taro balls are cooked until they float and then for 2 additional minutes to guarantee they are fully done.

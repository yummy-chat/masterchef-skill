# Caramel-Coated Taro

Cuisine: Common Home-Style Dish
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for All Seasons

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🥔 1 Lipu taro (about 700g, preferably large for a soft and fluffy texture)
🥔 About 50g potato starch (used in two portions)
🫒 Vegetable oil, as needed (enough for shallow frying)
🍬 About 150g white sugar
💧 About 150g water (mixed with sugar at a 1:1 ratio to make syrup)

## Kitchenware and Equipment
🥣 Stainless steel basin (for washing taro)
🪓 Cutting board
🔪 Kitchen knife
🧺 Bamboo basket (for draining and coating)
🥣 Small bowl (for mixing syrup)
🍳 Wok (for frying and making syrup)
🥄 Slotted spoon
🥄 Spatula

## Cooking Steps
1. Wash the mud off the taro skin, trim off the ends, and peel off the skin.
   **Note**: Must wash before peeling to prevent dirt from contaminating the flesh; the peeled surface oxidizes easily, so work quickly.
2. Cutting: First cut a flat side on the taro to stabilize it, then slice into about 1cm thick pieces, cut into strips, and finally into uniform diamond-shaped pieces.
   **Key**: The diamond shape maximizes taro usage and reduces waste.
3. Rinse the cut taro pieces in clean water to remove surface starch, then drain thoroughly.
   **Key**: Removing surface starch prevents the taro from turning black due to oxidation later.
4. Coating: Place the drained taro pieces in a bamboo basket. Sprinkle in half the potato starch and toss to coat. Lightly spray with a little water using a spray bottle, then add the remaining starch and toss again until each piece is evenly coated with a thin layer of starch.
   **Key**: Adding starch in stages and spraying water allows the taro surface to fully absorb moisture and starch, resulting in a white color after frying and sealing in moisture to prevent drying.
5. Frying: Pour vegetable oil into the wok and heat to around 170°C (340°F). Add the taro pieces, then reduce the heat to low and deep-fry gently.
   **Note**: Do not stir immediately after adding to the oil to prevent breakage. Keep the heat low throughout to avoid burning the outside while leaving the inside raw.
   **Timing**: First fry for about 2 minutes, then remove. Fry again for about 4 minutes until all taro pieces float, the exterior is firm, and they are slightly pale yellow.
6. Making the syrup: In a separate wok, pour in the mixture of white sugar and water. Heat over low heat.
   **Texture check**: After the sugar water boils, it will first show large bubbles → continue cooking until the syrup turns to dense small bubbles.
   **Key**: The syrup must reach the "dense small bubble" stage before adding the taro. Adding too early results in a syrup that is too thin and won't stick; too late, and it will become toffee or caramel.
7. Coating with sugar: Once the syrup reaches the correct stage, immediately add the fried taro. Turn off the heat and stir constantly with a spatula.
   **Key**: Continue stirring using the residual heat of the wok. As the temperature drops, the syrup will transform from a clear liquid to a white solid, evenly coating each piece of taro.
   **Texture check**: Stir until the taro has completely cooled and the sugar coating is fully formed and set.
8. Remove from the wok and plate. Serve and enjoy.

## Health Advice
Caramel-Coated Taro is rich in dietary fiber and complex carbohydrates, making it a suitable occasional dessert. However, due to the frying and sugar coating, it is high in calories. It is recommended to control the serving size and enjoy just a few pieces after a meal. The texture of the taro is soft and fluffy, making it suitable for the elderly and children in moderation, but they should be sure to chew thoroughly and eat slowly.

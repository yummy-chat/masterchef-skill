# Sesame Sauce Cold Skin Noodles
Cuisine: Shaanxi Cuisine
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Summer (April-August)

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🌾 Flour 500g
🥒 Cucumber appropriate amount (shredded for garnish)
🧂 Salt 5g (for the batter)
🧂 Alkaline water 2g (to increase chewiness)
🍶 Shaanxi fragrant vinegar 300g
🫙 Sesame paste appropriate amount (mixed with peanut butter in a 3:1 ratio)
🥜 Peanut butter appropriate amount (mixed with sesame paste in a 1:3 ratio)
🫒 Sesame oil appropriate amount
🫗 Light soy sauce appropriate amount
🌿 Cumin 20g
⭐ Star anise 10g
🌶️ Sichuan peppercorns 5g
🪵 Cinnamon stick 3g
🍃 Bay leaves 1g
🧅 Scallions 30g (for frying spice oil)
🫚 Ginger 30g (for frying spice oil) + appropriate amount of minced ginger (for making vinegar water)
🌶️ Chili oil appropriate amount (drizzled at the end)

## Cookware and Equipment
🍳 Wok
🥣 Stainless steel bowl
🪣 Strainer/Filter net
🥄 Spoon
🥘 Flat-bottomed metal steaming pan
🥟 Large steamer
⚖️ Heavy lid
🔪 Knife, cutting board
🍽️ Bowl

## Preparation Steps
### Making the Sesame Sauce
1. In a bowl, mix pure sesame paste and peanut butter in a 3:1 ratio. Add appropriate amounts of sesame oil and light soy sauce, stirring well.
2. Add a small amount of oil to a wok. Add 30g scallions, 30g ginger slices, and cumin, star anise, Sichuan peppercorns, cinnamon stick, and bay leaves. Fry over low heat until the scallions and ginger turn dry and yellow, then remove from heat.
3. Strain the spice oil and pour it into the sauce bowl. Stir in one direction until slightly thick and sticky.
**Note**: Use oil to mix the sauce instead of water for richer aroma and better shelf life; spices must be thoroughly fried to release their fragrance.

### Making the Vinegar Water
1. Soak cumin, star anise, cinnamon stick, Sichuan peppercorns, and bay leaves in 700g of boiling water to make spiced water.
2. Add a little oil to a wok, sauté minced ginger until fragrant, pour in 300g fragrant vinegar to release the aroma, then add the filtered spiced water. Bring to a boil, remove the minced ginger, and pour into a bowl to cool.
**Note**: Pure vinegar is too acidic and must be diluted with spiced water; the vinegar needs to be "cooked" with ginger to stimulate a milder aroma.

### Making the Cold Skin Noodle Batter
1. Add 2g alkaline water and 5g salt to 500g flour, mixing well.
2. **Must use cold water** and add gradually: first add a small amount of water and stir into a thick paste without lumps, then continue adding water while stirring until it becomes a thin, flowing batter. Let it rest for a moment.
**Key**: The initial addition of water should be minimal, and ensure no dry flour particles remain to avoid lumps in the batter later.

### Steaming the Cold Skin Noodles
1. Stir the rested batter well, ladle it into the flat-bottomed steaming pan, and swirl to spread evenly.
2. Place the pan onto the boiling water surface. Gently rotate it once to let the batter set from the heat, then place it flat.
3. Cover with a heavy lid and steam over high heat for about 3 minutes, until large bubbles appear on the surface, indicating doneness.
### Cooling and Cutting
1. Remove the pan immediately and cool it in cold water.
2. Lift the entire sheet from the edge, brush a little oil on the surface to prevent sticking.
3. Fold the sheet and cut into wide strips with a large knife.
### Assembling the Dish
1. Place the cut noodle strips into a bowl.
2. Add salt, the cooled vinegar water, a spoonful of sesame sauce, and a spoonful of chili oil in order. Finally, sprinkle shredded cucumber on top, and mix well.

## Health Advice
The golden ratio of sesame paste and peanut butter provides a rich flavor, while using spice oil to mix the sauce enhances the aroma and reduces oil usage. This dish is suitable when appetite is low, but those with sensitive stomachs should control the amount of chili.

# Spicy Lamb Spine (Málà Yáng Xiē Zi)
Cuisine: Beijing Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Winter (November-December)  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🥩 Lamb spine (lamb backbone), about 6 jin (3 kg), cut into pieces  
🌶️ Pixian doubanjiang (broad bean paste), appropriate amount (finely chopped)  
🫑 Green Sichuan peppercorns, appropriate amount  
🌶️ Red Sichuan peppercorns, appropriate amount  
🌶️ Dried chilies (Erjingtiao, Neihuang Xinyidai), appropriate amount  
🍂 Baizhi (Angelica dahurica), 2 slices  
🌳 Cinnamon sticks, a few small pieces  
🍊 Dried tangerine peel, a small amount  
⚪ White peppercorns, a small amount  
⚫ Black peppercorns, a small amount  
🧅 Scallions, appropriate amount (cut into large sections)  
🧄 Ginger, a large amount (some sliced for frying, some cut into large pieces)  
🧄 Garlic, appropriate amount (sliced)  
🍶 High-proof baijiu (Chinese liquor), appropriate amount  
🍶 Huangjiu (or cooking wine), a generous amount  
🥡 Light soy sauce, a generous amount  
🥡 Dark soy sauce, a small amount  
🧂 Salt, appropriate amount (slightly saltier than usual stir-fry)  

## Tools and Equipment  
⚖️ Kitchen scale  
🔪 Cutting board, chef's knife  
🪣 Large basin  
🥄 Slotted spoon  
🍳 Wok (also used as a stew pot)  
🫗 Fine mesh strainer  
🥄 Soup ladle  
🍲 Lid  
🥢 Chopsticks  

## Instructions  
1. Place the lamb spine pieces in a large basin. Add enough cold water to completely submerge them, along with an appropriate amount of high-proof baijiu. Soak for 2 to 4 hours.  
**Note**: Soaking helps effectively remove blood water; the baijiu enhances the deodorizing effect.  

2. After soaking, use your hands or scissors to remove any remaining blood vessels and blood clots from the lamb spine pieces. This is a crucial step for further removing the gamey smell.  
3. Place the prepared lamb spine into a pot with cold water. Add enough clean water and a little more high-proof baijiu. Bring to a boil to blanch. Ensure there is sufficient water so that all the scum can rise fully.  
4. Just as the water is about to boil and a large amount of scum has risen, gently stir and press the meat pieces with a slotted spoon to release the scum through friction. Then skim off all the foam, remove the lamb spine, and immediately rinse off any surface impurities with warm water.  
**Note**: Be sure to use warm water for rinsing to prevent the lamb fat from solidifying and becoming difficult to wash off.  

5. Place the Pixian doubanjiang on a cutting board and finely chop it. Set aside. Prepare all the spices: lightly rinse the green Sichuan peppercorns, red Sichuan peppercorns, and dried chilies with warm water, then drain. Mix the baizhi, cinnamon sticks, dried tangerine peel, white peppercorns, and black peppercorns together. Cut the scallions into sections, slice some of the ginger and cut the rest into large pieces, and slice the garlic.  
6. Pour more oil than usual for stir-frying into the wok. First, add some of the sliced ginger. Stir-fry over medium-low heat slowly until the edges of the ginger slices turn golden brown and curl, becoming slightly scorched. This step is an important source of the rich aroma for the broth.  
7. Add the green Sichuan peppercorns, red Sichuan peppercorns, dried chilies, and the large sections of scallion, ginger, and garlic. Stir-fry over medium heat until the sizzling sound diminishes, the moisture has evaporated, and the fragrance is released.  
8. Add the remaining dry spices like baizhi, cinnamon sticks, dried tangerine peel, white peppercorns, and black peppercorns. Continue stir-frying briefly to evenly heat all the spices.  
9. Push the spices to one side of the wok. Add the chopped Pixian doubanjiang to the exposed oil at the bottom. Stir-fry slowly over low heat until the red oil is released and the aroma is strong. If sticking occurs, reduce the heat further. This step determines the bright red color of the broth.  
10. Splash the huangjiu along the edge of the wok. Then add a generous amount of light soy sauce and a small amount of dark soy sauce. Stir-fry quickly to combine. Adding the liquid helps prevent the doubanjiang from sticking further.  
11. Continue stir-frying until the moisture from the huangjiu and light soy sauce has mostly evaporated, and the bottom of the wok shows a clear red oil without excess liquid – a "bright oil" state.  
12. Pour enough boiling water into the wok, stir, and bring to a rapid boil over high heat.  
13. Once the broth is boiling vigorously, add the blanched and rinsed lamb spine pieces. Ensure the water level completely covers the ingredients.  
14. Cover with a lid. Turn the heat to the lowest setting to maintain a gentle simmer. Slow-cook for 1 hour 40 minutes to 2 hours. Check periodically. The lamb is done when a pair of chopsticks can be easily inserted into the meat.  
**Note**: Slow simmering over low heat is key for the lamb to become tender and flavorful and for the broth to be free of gamey taste. Adding salt too early can toughen the meat; it's recommended to add other seasonings first and supplement with salt at the end.  

15. Uncover and taste the broth. Add salt as needed, aiming for a saltiness slightly stronger than a normal stir-fry. If the lamb is not yet tender enough, continue simmering, covered, over low heat. After turning off the heat, let the lamb spine soak in the broth for two hours to allow it to absorb more flavor.  

## Health Tips  
Lamb spine is rich in calcium and collagen. Eating it in winter can provide warming nourishment, suitable for people with cold hands and feet. However, the spicy broth is high in oil and sodium. It is recommended to pair it with refreshing vegetables to balance the diet. Those with gout or high blood pressure should consume it sparingly.

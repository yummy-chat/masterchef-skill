# Sakura Wagashi (Cherry Blossom Japanese Confectionery)
Cuisine: Other
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Spring (January-April)

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🫘 Red beans 80g (for filling)
🫘 White kidney beans 150g (for nerikiri outer skin)
🍚 Glutinous rice flour 6g (for gyūhi)
🍬 Granulated sugar 90g (40g for filling, added in two batches; 50g for nerikiri)
🧂 Salt 1 small pinch
🎨 Food coloring (red, yellow) a small amount
💧 Water as needed (for soaking beans, cooking beans, and mixing rice dough)

## Kitchen Tools & Equipment
🥣 Glass bowl
🥅 Strainer
🍳 Non-stick pan
🥄 Silicone spatula
🌀 Blender
📡 Microwave
⚖️ Digital scale
📦 Plastic wrap
🫗 Fine mesh sieve
🥘 Small saucepan
🔧 Chrysanthemum needle (wagashi shaping tool)
🔨 Triangular stick (wagashi shaping tool)

## Preparation Steps
1. **Make red bean paste filling**
   - Soak red beans in advance, then drain the water. Add enough fresh water, bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to low heat and cook for 1-2 hours until soft and mushy.
   - Place the cooked red beans on a fine mesh sieve and press firmly through the sieve to obtain a smooth red bean puree.
   - Heat a non-stick pan over low heat, pour in the red bean puree. Add a small pinch of salt and 20g of granulated sugar, stirring continuously.
   - Once the sugar has completely melted and the bean paste has thickened, add the remaining 20g of granulated sugar and continue stirring.
   - Stir until the bean paste can form a ball and slide as a whole in the pan without sticking to the sides. Remove from heat, cover immediately with plastic wrap touching the surface while hot, and refrigerate for later use.
   - After cooling, divide into 15g balls and roll them round.
   **Key point**: No oil throughout the process; stir constantly to prevent sticking; the paste is done when it holds together and doesn't stick to the pan.

2. **Make nerikiri base white bean paste**
   - Soak white kidney beans for at least 6 hours, then peel off the skins.
   - Put the peeled beans in a pot, add enough water, bring to a boil, then reduce to low heat and cook for about 45 minutes. Add 50g of granulated sugar and mix well.
   - Pour the beans along with the cooking liquid into a blender and blend into a smooth puree.
   - Return the white bean puree to the non-stick pan, stir continuously over low heat until moisture has evaporated and it can form a ball.
   - Remove from the pan, place in the microwave, and heat on high in short intervals, stirring in between, to further dry out the white bean paste.
   **Key point**: White kidney beans must be fully soaked and peeled; be patient during the stirring process to ensure the moisture content is low enough.

3. **Make the nerikiri skin**
   - Take 6g of glutinous rice flour, gradually add cold water, and knead into a soft small dough ball.
   - Place the small dough ball into boiling water and cook until it floats to the surface. Remove; this is the gyūhi.
   - Add the hot gyūhi to the cooked white bean paste. Once it is cool enough to handle, knead thoroughly with your hands until the dough is smooth, soft, pliable, and not sticky.
   **Key point**: The gyūhi must be fully cooked; knead it in completely and evenly for a fine texture without lumps.

4. **Color and portion**
   - Divide the nerikiri dough into several portions: keep one portion white; add a small amount of red food coloring to one portion and knead into pink; take a very small portion, add a small amount of yellow food coloring, and knead into a light yellow.
   - Wrap each portion immediately with plastic wrap to prevent the surface from drying out.

5. **Wrap the filling**
   - Take about 25g of the pink nerikiri dough, roll it into a ball, and flatten it.
   - Place one red bean paste filling ball in the center. Use the space between your thumb and index finger to gently push the outer skin upward, sealing the opening and rolling it into a smooth ball, with the seam side facing down.
   **Tip**: Be gentle when sealing, keeping the outer skin thickness even.

6. **Shape the cherry blossom**
   - Gently flatten the filled dough ball.
   - Use the chrysanthemum needle to prick a small hole in the very center as a guide point.
   - Using the ridged edge of the triangular stick, press a crease from the edge of the dough towards the center hole. Rotate the dough about 72° and press another crease, repeating to form 5 petals.
   - Use your finger to gently press a small dimple in the center of each petal.
   - Use the ridged edge of the triangular stick to lightly press the characteristic cherry blossom notch at the tip of each petal.
   - Use the round end of the triangular stick to press down into the center hole, forming a more defined stamen pit.
   - Use the chrysanthemum needle to lightly scratch several radiating thin lines on the petals near the center.
   **Key point**: Work in order – mark the center, press the petal divisions, shape the petals, create the notches, form the stamen pit, and add texture lines.

7. **Make and place the stamens**
   - Take a small piece of the yellow nerikiri dough and press it through a fine mesh sieve using your fingers, so it extrudes through the holes, forming thin, natural-looking stamen pieces.
   - Carefully transfer an appropriate amount of these stamen pieces using a toothpick and place them into the pit in the center of the wagashi.
   **Tip**: Using the sieve texture makes it easy to create realistic-looking stamens.

## Health Advice
This wagashi has a relatively high sugar content but is low in fat. It is best enjoyed occasionally as a tea-time treat. Diabetic patients should control their intake. The bean paste is rich in dietary fiber, which aids digestion, but the glutinous rice skin is not easy to digest. The elderly and children should try it in moderation.

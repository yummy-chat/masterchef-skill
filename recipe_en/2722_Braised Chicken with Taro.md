# Braised Chicken with Taro
Cuisine: Sichuan Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Autumn (September-October)  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🐔 1 free-range chicken (cleaned, cut into evenly sized pieces)  
🥔 Taro, as needed (choose small red-skinned taro; cut larger ones into pieces, peeled)  
🌶️ Pickled chilies, as needed (cut into short sections)  
🫚 Pickled ginger, as needed (sliced)  
🧄 Garlic, as needed (peeled, whole or halved)  
🫒 1 tablespoon rapeseed oil  
🧈 1 tablespoon lard  
🌶️ Dried chilies, as needed  
🫑 Sichuan peppercorns, as needed  
🫘 Homemade "Yin" broad bean paste, 1 tablespoon  
🫘 Aged "Yang" broad bean paste, 1 tablespoon  
🍬 Rock sugar, as needed  
⭐ Star anise, a few  
🪵 Cinnamon stick, as needed  
🍂 Sand ginger (Kaempferia galanga), as needed  
🍃 Bay leaves, as needed  
🌶️ Chili powder, as needed  
🍾 Soy sauce, as needed  
🧂 Salt, as needed  
🧂 MSG, as needed  
🧂 Chicken bouillon, as needed  
🌿 White pepper powder, as needed  
🍶 White vinegar, as needed (for blanching taro)  
🧅 Garlic sprouts, as needed (optional, for aroma before serving)  

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment  
🔪 Chef's knife  
🪓 Cutting board  
🍳 Wok (large iron wok)  
🥄 Soup ladle  
🥄 Slotted spoon  
🔥 Gas stove  

## Preparation Steps  
1.  Kill and clean the free-range chicken, then chop it into evenly sized pieces. Peel the taro, keep small ones whole, and cut larger ones into chunks. Cut pickled chilies into short sections, slice the pickled ginger, and peel the garlic. Set aside.  
2.  Heat the wok, add rapeseed oil and lard. When the oil reaches 60% heat, add the chicken pieces. Stir-fry over medium-high heat repeatedly until the chicken is completely dry of moisture, the oil in the wok becomes clear, and the surface of the chicken pieces is slightly browned.  
**Key Point**: The moisture must be thoroughly evaporated; otherwise, the dish will have a strong, unpleasant smell and poor texture. The combination of oils provides a more complex fatty aroma.  
3.  Add dried chilies and Sichuan peppercorns in order, stir-frying until a pungent aroma bursts forth. Then add pickled chilies, pickled ginger, and garlic, stir-frying until the lactic acid fermented aroma is released. Finally, add the "Yin" and "Yang" broad bean pastes. Reduce the heat to medium-low and stir-fry until red oil is extracted and the sauce becomes fragrant and slightly crispy.  
**Key Point**: The aromatics must be added in sequence. The combination of the two types of broad bean paste is the core of the flavor profile.  
4.  Add rock sugar, star anise, cinnamon stick, sand ginger, bay leaves, and chili powder. Stir-fry evenly. Splash soy sauce along the edge of the wok to enhance umami. Then, add enough hot water at once to submerge the chicken pieces. Bring to a boil over high heat. Season with salt, MSG, chicken bouillon, and white pepper powder. Reduce the heat to medium-low and simmer.  
5.  In a separate pot, bring water to a boil, add a little white vinegar, and blanch the taro pieces. When the surface mucilage is released, skim off any foam. Remove the taro and drain thoroughly.  
**Key Point**: White vinegar and blanching help remove the mucilage from taro, preventing the broth from becoming cloudy during subsequent cooking.  
6.  Determine the timing for adding the taro based on the age of the chicken. If the chicken is older, simmer it for 10-20 minutes before adding the taro. If the chicken is younger, the taro can be added together with the chicken. Continue cooking over medium-low heat until the taro is completely tender and soft, and the sauce has reduced to a thick, semi-dry consistency.  
**Key Point**: Do not reduce the sauce too much; keep some to mix with rice. The taro should be cooked until it is "pa" (very soft) but not falling apart.  
7.  Just before serving, sprinkle in the garlic sprout sections. Stir-fry briefly until fragrant, then transfer to a serving dish.  

## Health Advice  
Taro provides dietary fiber, and chicken supplies high-quality protein, making this dish an excellent nourishing choice for autumn. However, pickled chilies and broad bean paste are high in sodium; individuals with high blood pressure should control their salt intake. Taro has a relatively high calorie density, so those on a weight loss plan should be mindful of portion sizes.

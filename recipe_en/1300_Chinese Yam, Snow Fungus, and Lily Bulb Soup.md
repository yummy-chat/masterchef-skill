# Chinese Yam, Snow Fungus, and Lily Bulb Soup
Cuisine: Common Home Cooking
Difficulty Level: ⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Autumn (September-October)

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🥔 1 Chinese yam (peeled and cut into rolling knife chunks)
🍄 1 snow fungus (fresh: tear into small florets directly; dried: soak in advance to rehydrate)
🌸 Appropriate amount of fresh lily bulb (tear into small petals and wash clean)
🫘 Appropriate amount of longan
🫘 8 red dates
🫘 Appropriate amount of goji berries
🧂 A few drops of white vinegar (for soaking the yam)
🍬 A small handful of rock sugar (add before removing from heat)

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment
🔪 Chef's knife
🥣 Glass bowl
🍲 Clear glass pot
🥄 Soup ladle

## Preparation Steps
1. Wear gloves when peeling the Chinese yam, then cut into rolling knife chunks.
   **Note**: Gloves must be worn to prevent skin itching caused by yam mucilage.
2. Soak the cut yam in water with a few drops of white vinegar to prevent oxidation and blackening.
3. Tear the fresh lily bulb into small petals, wash clean, and remove any dirt or spoiled parts.
4. Process the snow fungus: tear dried snow fungus into small florets, soak in water with a pinch of sugar to rehydrate, then wash clean; for fresh snow fungus, simply tear into small florets directly.
   **Key point**: Adding sugar when soaking uses osmotic pressure to accelerate water absorption by dried snow fungus.
5. Pour water into the pot, then add the yam, snow fungus, and lily bulb. The water should cover all ingredients.
6. Add the longan, red dates, and goji berries to enhance flavor and nourishing effects.
7. Bring to a boil over high heat, then turn to low heat and simmer gently for 20 minutes, until the soup is lightly boiling, the ingredients are tender, and the snow fungus releases its gelatin.
8. Before removing from heat, add a small handful of rock sugar and stir until dissolved. Serve and enjoy.

## Health Advice
This soup nourishes the lungs and beautifies the skin, making it suitable for dry autumn weather. Diabetics can reduce the rock sugar or substitute with a sugar alternative. The key is for the snow fungus to release its gelatin, which requires a slow simmer over low heat for a soft, sticky texture.

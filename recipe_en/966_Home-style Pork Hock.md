# Home-style Pork Hock  
Cuisine: Sichuan  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🐖 1 pork front hock (about 1500g, skin-on, preferably front hock)  
🫚 Ginger, appropriate amount (partially smashed, partially minced)  
🧅 1 scallion (cut into small sections)  
🌱 1 small bunch of spring onion (chopped into scallions)  
🧄 A few cloves of garlic (smashed and minced)  
🌶️ 1 small handful of dried chili peppers  
🌸 Sichuan peppercorns, appropriate amount (used in two batches)  
🌿 2 bay leaves  
🍶 50g cooking wine  
🧂 Salt, appropriate amount  
🧂 10g chicken bouillon (for blanching) + a pinch (for the sauce)  
🥄 30g light soy sauce (for blanching) + a little (for the sauce)  
🥄 Dark soy sauce, appropriate amount (for coloring)  
🌶️ 60g red pickled chili peppers (chopped)  
🫘 Red oil chili bean paste, appropriate amount (chopped)  
🫒 Rapeseed oil, appropriate amount (for the sauce base)  
🧈 Lard, appropriate amount (for fragrance)  
🍬 2g sugar  
🌿 1g white pepper powder  
🥣 Water starch, appropriate amount (for thickening)  
🫒 A little scallion oil  

## Kitchenware and Equipment  
🪓 Cleaver  
🔪 Chef’s knife  
🔥 Blowtorch  
🍳 Large wok  
♨️ Pressure cooker  
🥄 Slotted spoon / large ladle  
🥣 Bowls / basins  

## Cooking Steps  
1. Use a cleaver to chop the pork hock along the joint, then use a chef’s knife to cut into the meat side of the hock to expose the tendon meat.  
2. Use a blowtorch to singe the skin of the hock until the surface shrinks and slightly chars, to remove the skin’s odor.  
3. Soak the singed hock in approximately 40°C warm water for 3 minutes. Remove and scrape off the black char marks on the skin with a knife, then rinse clean and set aside.  
4. Place the smashed ginger, scallion sections, dried chili peppers, part of the Sichuan peppercorns, and the bay leaves into a bowl as braising aromatics.  
5. Place the minced ginger, minced garlic, chopped scallions, chopped red pickled chili peppers, and chopped red oil chili bean paste into separate small bowls as sauce ingredients.  
6. Fill a large wok with about half a wok of water, add the hock and all the braising aromatics. Add cooking wine, salt, 10g chicken bouillon, 30g light soy sauce, and an appropriate amount of dark soy sauce.  
7. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to low heat and skim off any foam from the surface.  
8. Transfer the hock and broth into a pressure cooker, making sure to place the hock skin-side up. Once pressurized, cook over medium heat for about 40 minutes.  
If no pressure cooker is available, simmer over low heat for about 2 hours until tender.  
9. While the hock is cooking, heat another wok with rapeseed oil and lard until 60% hot. Add the remaining Sichuan peppercorns, minced ginger, and minced garlic, and stir-fry until fragrant. Then add the chopped chili bean paste and red pickled chili peppers, and stir-fry until the red oil releases.  
10. Add an appropriate amount of water to the wok and bring to a boil. Season with a little salt, 2g sugar, a pinch of chicken bouillon, a little light soy sauce, and 1g white pepper powder.  
11. Once the sauce is boiling again, add an appropriate amount of water starch to thicken, stirring until the sauce is glossy and thickened.  
12. Add the chopped scallions and stir-fry evenly. Just before turning off the heat, drizzle in a little scallion oil to create a rich, thick sauce.  
13. Remove the cooked hock and plate it. Evenly pour the sauce over the hock and serve.  

## Health Tips  
Pork hock is rich in collagen, making it suitable for those with dry skin or joint discomfort. However, it is high in calories, so people on a diet are advised to limit each serving to 150 grams. Those with high blood pressure should reduce the amount of salt and chili bean paste, and pair the dish with stir-fried vegetables to balance the fat content.

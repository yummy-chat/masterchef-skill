# Squirrel-Shaped Mandarin Fish
Cuisine: Jiangsu Cuisine  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🐟 Mandarin fish 1 (about 1.5 jin)  
🥕 Carrot 1 small section (round slices, discarded after frying)  
🫛 Peas appropriate amount (for garnish)  
🧅 Scallions appropriate amount (large pieces for marinating, a little minced)  
🧄 Ginger appropriate amount (large pieces for marinating, a little minced)  
🧂 Salt 8g (divided: 3g for marinating, 5g for sauce)  
🍶 High-proof liquor appropriate amount  
🥫 Ketchup 60g  
💧 Water 200mL  
🍬 White sugar 100g  
🍾 White vinegar 70mL  
🌾 Dried starch appropriate amount (for coating)  
🥣 Wet starch appropriate amount (for battering)  
🥣 Water starch 1 tablespoon (for thickening)  
🫒 Hot oil 1 tablespoon (for final drizzle)  

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment  
🔪 Chef's knife  
🪓 Cutting board  
🥣 Glass bowl  
🍳 Wok  
🥄 Soup spoon  
🥄 Strainer  
🥢 Chopsticks  
🍽️ Plate  

## Preparation Steps  
1. Cut behind the fish's pectoral fin to remove the head and set aside.  
2. Cut close to the bone behind the dorsal fin, push flat toward the tail, and remove the two whole fillets, keeping them attached at the tail.  
3. Remove the fine bones from the belly side of the fillets, ensuring they are boneless.  
4. First, slice the fillets diagonally to the skin at a wide angle, spacing each cut evenly; then turn 90° and cut vertically down to the skin, creating a crosshatch pattern.  
**Note**: The diagonal cuts determine the length of the "squirrel fur"; do not cut through the skin in either step.  
5. Place the fillets and head in a bowl, add 3g salt, high-proof liquor, and large pieces of scallion and ginger. Mix well and marinate for 10 minutes.  
6. First, coat the marinated fillets evenly in wet starch, ensuring every crevice is covered; then transfer to dried starch, sprinkle and toss until each piece is evenly coated and separated, forming a pinecone-like shape.  
**Note**: The coating sequence is wet first, then dry. Ensure the dry starch is fully coated and shake off excess.  
7. Insert a round carrot slice into the fish's mouth to prop it open. Lift the fillets and shake off excess dry starch.  
8. Heat oil in a wok to 50–60% hot. Fry the fish head, continuously ladling hot oil over it, until golden. Remove and drain.  
9. Hold the fish tail, use chopsticks to grip the front of the fillets; fold the tail upward from below to open the pattern. Lower into the oil while maintaining the folded position, using chopsticks to hold the center for shaping. Fry for about 4–5 minutes until lightly golden and set, then remove.  
**Note**: After folding open, use chopsticks to help maintain the shape.  
10. Reheat the oil to 50% hot, fry the fish body again until the skin is crispy. Remove and drain. Arrange the head and body on a plate.  
11. In another wok, leave a base oil, sauté minced scallion and ginger until fragrant. Add ketchup and stir-fry until red oil appears. Add water, sugar, 5g salt, and white vinegar. Bring to a boil, then thicken with water starch until it coats a spoon.  
12. Add blanched peas to the sauce, drizzle in one tablespoon of hot oil, and stir quickly. Turn off the heat, and while hot, pour evenly over the fish. A sizzling sound should be heard.  
**Note**: The final drizzle of hot oil enhances gloss and aroma; the sauce must be poured while hot.  

## Health Advice  
This squirrel-shaped mandarin fish is high in sugar and oil. It is recommended as a festive dish to enjoy occasionally. Those controlling sugar intake should use the sauce with caution. Mandarin fish is rich in high-quality protein, but frying significantly increases calories. Pair with light vegetables for a more balanced meal.

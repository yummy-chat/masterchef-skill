# Northeast Chinese Braised Pot
Cuisine: Northeast Chinese
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: Winter (November-December)

## Ingredients and Seasonings
🦴 Pork leg bones 3-4 jin
🥩 Pork spine/shank bones 4 jin
🐷 Pig trotters, pork hocks, pork ears, pig tails (appropriate amounts each, need to be burned)
🧅 Scallions 3-4 (whole, flattened)
🫚 Ginger about 200g (flattened)
🧂 Light soy sauce 500g
🍶 Rice wine 500g
🍬 Braised sugar caramel 300g
🧴 Dark soy sauce about 200ml
🧂 Salt (adjust at the end, until "excessively salty")
🌿 Homemade spice sachet: Sichuan pepper 20g, star anise 30g, cinnamon 5g, baizhi 3g (optional)
📦 Pre-made spice sachets 5 small packs
🫒 Fried ginger slices about 200g

## Kitchenware and Equipment
🪣 Large capacity stainless steel stockpot (about 22L)
🥘 Large wok
🔲 Metal steaming rack/mat (to prevent sticking at the bottom)
🥄 Slotted spoon
🕸️ Fine mesh strainer
⚖️ Kitchen scale
🔪 Chef's knife and cutting board
🎬 Plastic wrap
🧱 Heavy object (such as a cutting board, for pressing and shaping ear rolls)

## Steps
1. **Prepare the bones**
   - Place leg bones, spine bones, and shank bones in cold water, blanch, skim off the foam, then remove and rinse.
   - After blanching, fry the spine and shank bones in plenty of oil until the surface is dehydrated and golden brown; fry the ginger slices at the same time.
**Note**: Blanching must thoroughly remove blood foam to prevent the sauce from spoiling; frying the bones and ginger enhances the broth's rich aroma.

2. **Prepare the spices**
   - Weigh Sichuan pepper, star anise, cinnamon, and baizhi according to the ratio, place in a gauze bag to make a homemade spice sachet.
   - Place the homemade sachet and pre-made sachets in cold water, bring to a boil, cook for 30 seconds to 1 minute, remove, and rinse.
**Note**: Pre-boiling the spice sachets removes "medicinal flavor" and dust, preventing the broth from becoming too pungent.

3. **Start the broth**
   - Add 20 jin of boiling water to the stockpot, place the steaming rack at the bottom, add leg bones, fried spine and shank bones, fried ginger slices, flattened scallions and ginger, and all spice sachets.
   - Bring to a boil over high heat.
4. **Season**
   - Once the water is boiling again, add light soy sauce, rice wine, and sugar caramel successively; stir to combine.
   - Gradually add dark soy sauce to adjust the color, based on the broth's hue, adding and observing as you go.
**Note**: The caramel provides flavor, while dark soy sauce is mainly responsible for color.

5. **Simmer the broth**
   - Taste for saltiness, then add salt until it is "excessively salty"—this is key for flavor infusion.
   - Cover the pot and keep it at a rolling boil for 90 minutes.
**Note**: Salt should be added all at once at the end; boiling allows the flavors from bones and spices to fully release.

6. **Clarify the broth**
   - After cooking, use a fine mesh strainer to thoroughly remove all residue, ensuring the broth is clear.
**Note**: The spice sachets and ginger can be saved for reuse; thorough cleaning is essential for reusing the broth and for the final product's appearance.

7. **Prepare ingredients for braising**
   - Use a blowtorch or open flame to burn the skin of all skin-on ingredients until charred black, then soak in warm water and scrub clean.
   - For pig trotters, cut a slit between the two toes; for pork hocks, if making hock rolls, debone and wrap tightly with a net.
**Note**: Burning the skin is a crucial step to remove gamey odors; it must be done thoroughly.

8. **Braise the ingredients**
   - Reheat the cleaned braising broth to a boil, add all prepared ingredients; if the broth is insufficient, top up with boiling water.
   - Once the broth is boiling again, start timing, cook for 40 minutes, then turn off the heat. Soak the ingredients in the broth overnight.
**Note**: The essence of braising lies in "soaking" rather than prolonged cooking; the residual heat cooks and flavors the ingredients while keeping their shape intact and texture chewy. Different ingredients can be added in batches; large hocks can be returned to the pot separately at the end.

9. **Final return to pot**
   - After soaking overnight, pig trotters, pig ears, and pig tails can be removed directly.
   - If large hocks are not fully tender, remove them separately, bring the broth to a boil, turn off the heat, return the hocks, cover, and let them steep until they fall off the bone.
**Note**: The return to pot follows the same principle: "boil the broth, then turn off the heat, using residual heat to steep," avoiding prolonged cooking that can toughen the meat and cloud the broth.

## Health Advice
This braised pot is rich in collagen and calcium, making it suitable for winter nourishment. However, due to its high salt and fat content, it is advisable for those with high blood pressure or those on a weight-loss diet to consume in moderation. Pair it with light vegetables or whole grains to balance dietary fiber and sodium intake.

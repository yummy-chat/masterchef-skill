# Large Mooncake
Cuisine: Other  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Autumn (September-October)  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🥚 Salted egg yolks 20 pieces  
🍠 Taro appropriate amount (steamed and mashed into taro paste)  
🍚 Glutinous rice flour 120g  
🌽 Cornstarch 20g  
🥛 Milk 260g  
🍬 White sugar 40g (for mochi) + appropriate amount (for taro paste)  
🧈 Butter 20g (for mochi) + appropriate amount (for taro paste)  
🌾 All-purpose flour 427g  
🍯 Inverted sugar syrup 294g  
🧪 Lye water 11g  
🥜 Peanut oil 94g  
🥓 Pork floss 60g  
🥗 Mayonnaise 60g  
🧂 Salt appropriate amount (for preparing salt water, ratio 1:20)  
🍶 Baijiu (white liquor) appropriate amount (spray on salted egg yolks to remove odor)  
🥚 Egg yolk 1 piece (for brushing the surface, mix with a small spoon of water)  

## Kitchen Tools and Equipment  
🥣 Glass bowl  
🔥 Oven (needs to be preheated to 220°C)  
♨️ Steamer or steaming pot  
🍳 Non-stick pan  
🌀 Stand mixer  
🔧 Handheld electric mixer (with dough hook attachment)  
🥖 Rolling pin  
🖐️ Large mooncake mold (approximately 500g)  
💧 Spray bottle  
🫙 Plastic wrap  

## Preparation Steps  
1. **Prepare the salted egg yolks**  
   Soak the salted egg yolks in salt water for 3 minutes, remove and drain, spray the surface with baijiu, and bake in a preheated oven until oil seeps out and they are fully cooked.  

2. **Make the mochi**  
   Mix the glutinous rice flour, cornstarch, milk, and 40g of white sugar until well combined. Strain the mixture into a bowl, cover with plastic wrap, poke holes in it, and steam until fully cooked.  
   While hot, add 20g of butter and beat with an electric mixer until smooth and stretchy.  
   **Key point**: The mochi batter is intentionally thin to prevent the finished product from hardening.  

3. **Make the taro paste filling**  
   Peel and cut the taro into chunks, steam until tender, then mash with milk and an appropriate amount of white sugar. Transfer to a stand mixer and beat until smooth.  
   Pour the mixture into a non-stick pan, add an appropriate amount of butter, and stir-fry over low heat continuously until the filling comes together into a ball.  
   **Note**: Stir constantly during the frying process, and make sure to fry until it forms a cohesive mass.  

4. **Make the dough**  
   Mix the inverted sugar syrup, lye water, and peanut oil together, stir until fully emulsified, then sift in the all-purpose flour and mix to form a dough.  
   Let the dough rest and relax for 1-2 hours, avoiding excessive kneading.  

5. **Make the pork floss and egg yolk filling**  
   Crush the baked salted egg yolks, then combine with pork floss and mayonnaise, and mix evenly.  

6. **Assemble the filling ball**  
   Wrap from the inside out in the order: “pork floss and egg yolk → mochi → taro paste.”  
   First, flatten the pork floss and egg yolk filling, place the mochi on top, and use plastic wrap to help wrap it into a ball. Then flatten the taro paste and wrap it around this ball.  
   **Key point**: Since the mochi is thin, it must be placed at the very center, so first wrap the mochi with the pork floss and egg yolk filling.  

7. **Wrap the mooncake**  
   Divide the rested dough into two portions, and roll each into a round sheet larger than the filling.  
   Place the filling ball on one sheet of dough, cover with the other sheet, and use plastic wrap to help seal the edges, fully encasing the filling.  

8. **Press the mold**  
   Evenly coat the mooncake blank with dry flour, place it into a large mold that has been dusted with dry flour, and use a rolling pin to forcefully roll and press the top of the mold, ensuring the patterns are clear, then unmold onto a baking sheet.  
   **Key point**: The large mold cannot be pressed directly; a rolling pin must be used to roll it out to fill the patterns properly.  

9. **Bake**  
   Spray a fine mist of water on the surface of the mooncake blank, place it into an oven preheated to 220°C (actual baking temperature around 190°C), and bake until the surface is nicely browned.  
   Remove and let cool slightly until warm, optionally brush with a thin layer of egg yolk water, then bake again at 145°C until golden brown.  
   **Note**: Baking temperature and time should be flexibly adjusted based on browning; brushing with egg yolk water is optional.  

10. **Cooling and storage**  
    Place the baked mooncakes on a wire rack to cool completely, then seal in an airtight container and let rest at room temperature to allow the oil to redistribute (huitan).  
    They are ready to eat the next day; the mochi will remain soft overnight. Taro paste tends to harden when refrigerated, so it is recommended to store at room temperature.  

## Health Advice  
This mooncake combines taro paste, mochi, pork floss, and salted egg yolks, making it high in carbohydrates and fats. It is recommended to cut into small pieces and share, consuming no more than 1/4 at a time to avoid blood sugar spikes and excessive calorie intake. Suitable as a dessert for gatherings, but those with weak digestive systems should pair it with strong tea to aid digestion.

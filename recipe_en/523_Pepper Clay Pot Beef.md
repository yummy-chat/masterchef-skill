# Pepper Clay Pot Beef
Cuisine: Cantonese
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐
Seasonal Recommendation: All seasons

## Ingredients & Seasonings
🥩 Beef (from the shoulder area, with little sinew and some fat, cut into coin-thick slices)
🧅 Small shallots (or onion, scallion, for the bottom layer)
🫚 Ginger (cut into small pieces, for the bottom layer)
🧄 Garlic (some for the bottom layer, some minced for garnishing)
🌿 Scallions (chopped, for garnishing)
🧂 Salt (for marinating and final seasoning)
⚫ Black pepper (coarsely ground, plenty, for marinating)
🦪 Oyster sauce (for marinating)
🫒 Sesame oil (or peanut oil, mature pressed rapeseed oil, for marinating)
🌽 Cornstarch (a small amount, added last during marinating)
🍶 Yellow rice wine (or low-alcohol wine like Guangdong rice wine, splashed in during cooking)

## Cookware & Equipment
🫕 Clay pot
🔪 Chef's knife
🪵 Cutting board
🥣 Stainless steel bowl (for marinating)
🧂 Pepper grinder (for fresh-ground black pepper)
🔥 Gas stove
🥄 Spoon

## Instructions
1. Select beef with little sinew and some fat. Cut into slices about the thickness of a coin. Do not wash the beef; if cleaned, squeeze out excess water thoroughly.
   **Key Point**: Slices should be of even thickness for consistent cooking. A bit of fat adds aromatic richness.

2. Cut shallots, ginger, and garlic into small pieces. Also prepare some minced garlic and chopped scallions.
   **Note**: Cut aromatics to a moderate size to avoid burning when used as a base layer.

3. Grind a generous amount of black pepper. Add the pepper, salt, oyster sauce, and aromatic oil (e.g., sesame oil) to the beef. Use your hands to mix thoroughly, then add a small amount of cornstarch and continue mixing until the meat is lightly coated. Spread the marinated beef evenly on a plate, stacking no more than two to three slices thick.
   **Key Point**: Freshly ground black pepper has a stronger aroma. Add cornstarch last to lock in moisture and create a tender texture. Thinly spread the beef on the plate to prevent sticking.

4. Dry-heat the clay pot over medium-high heat for about 2 minutes, until you feel noticeable heat when placing your hand above it. Add oil to coat the pot, then arrange the shallots, ginger, and garlic pieces as a base layer.
   **Key Point**: The clay pot must be fully preheated to avoid cracking from sudden temperature changes. Use enough oil to prevent sticking and enhance aroma.

5. Reduce the heat to low, then slide the beef slices into the pot in a single, even layer. Cover with the lid, then pour a splash of yellow rice wine around the rim of the lid.
   **Key Point**: Use low heat to prevent the beef from shrinking too quickly. Pour wine along the lid's edge so it vaporizes upon contact with the hot pot, creating a steaming effect.

6. Turn the heat to high until steam rises from the lid, then reduce to medium heat and simmer for about 8 minutes. Listen to the sound inside the pot: a continuous bubbling sound means the liquid is sufficient; if you hear an oil-sizzling sound, the liquid has evaporated—immediately add a small splash of wine or boiling water along the pot's edge.
   **Key Point**: Maintain medium heat to ensure the beef is fully cooked yet juicy. Monitor moisture by sound and replenish liquid promptly to prevent burning.

7. Once the time is up, remove the lid. Sprinkle with chopped scallions and minced garlic. Use residual heat or a brief low heat to release their aromas. Taste and adjust seasoning with a pinch of salt if needed.
   **Key Point**: Add scallions and garlic at the end to preserve their fresh green color and pungent raw flavor. Final salt adjustment is optional, as oyster sauce already provides saltiness.

## Health Tips
Beef is high in protein and low in fat. Black pepper, ginger, and garlic aid digestion and warm the stomach, making this dish ideal for warming up in autumn and winter or for supporting muscle gain during workouts. Use salt sparingly during marination, as oyster sauce already contains salt. Strictly follow the heat control to avoid overcooking the beef.

# Pan-Seared Steak
Cuisine: Other  
Cooking Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons  

## Ingredients and Seasonings  
🥩 Steak, as needed (cuts like sirloin, ribeye, or fillet, about 2 cm thick)  
🧂 Salt, as needed  
🧂 Black pepper, as needed  
🫒 High smoke point cooking oil, as needed  
🧈 Butter, as needed  
🌿 Thyme, as needed  
🌿 Rosemary, as needed  
🧄 Garlic, as needed (crushed or sliced)  

## Cookware and Equipment  
🍳 Heavy-bottomed frying pan (cast iron, stainless steel, or copper)  
🌡️ Probe thermometer  
🥄 Tongs  
🥄 Basting spoon  
🧻 Paper towels  
🪵 Cutting board (wooden or plastic)  
🍽️ Warm plate or baking tray  
🧈 Aluminum foil  

## Steps  
1. **Thaw the Steak**  
   - **Preferred method: Slow thaw in the refrigerator**: Keep the original packaging and place in the refrigerator. Standard thickness takes 12–18 hours, while thick cuts take about 24 hours.  
   - **Alternative method: Quick thaw at room temperature**: Keep the packaging and soak in room-temperature water, completing in about 1 hour.  
   - **Note**: Avoid microwaving to thaw, as it creates uneven internal temperatures.  
2. **Prepare the Steak**  
   - Thoroughly pat the steak dry with paper towels—this is key for forming a crust.  
   - Evenly sprinkle salt on both sides. Black pepper can be added now or after searing.  
   - **Note**: No need to marinate for long; cook promptly after seasoning.  
3. **Preheat the Pan and Oil**  
   - Heat the heavy-bottomed pan over high heat.  
   - **Check pan temperature**: For cast iron, hold your hand a few inches above for 3 seconds—it should feel noticeably hot. For stainless steel or copper, drop a few drops of water; if they bead up and roll quickly, the temperature is right.  
   - Pour enough high smoke point oil to coat the pan bottom, swirling to spread evenly.  
   - **Note**: The pan must be sufficiently hot to trigger the Maillard reaction.  
4. **Sear the Steak**  
   - Lay the steak flat in the pan—you should hear a distinct sizzle. Sear over medium-high heat for 30–60 seconds until a dark brown crust forms.  
   - Flip and sear the other side until browned.  
   - After initial browning on both sides, reduce to medium-low heat. Add butter, thyme, rosemary, and garlic cloves.  
   - Use a spoon to continuously baste the steak with the melted herb butter for about 1 minute.  
   - **Optional**: For sirloin steak, sear the fat cap by standing it on its side to render extra fat and boost flavor.  
   - **Note**: Avoid flipping too often—sear on high heat to set the crust, then lower heat to prevent burning. Flip once or multiple times as needed for even color and doneness control.  
5. **Check Doneness and Remove from Pan**  
   - **Most accurate method**: Insert a probe thermometer into the thickest part of the steak.  
     - Rare: 49–54°C  
     - Medium-rare: 55–60°C  
     - Medium: 61–68°C  
     - Well-done: 71–77°C  
   - **Touch method**: Press the center of the steak and compare to the firmness of different parts of your hand—this is subjective, so pair with a thermometer for accuracy.  
   - **Remove early**: Account for carryover cooking by removing the steak about 5°C before the target temperature.  
   - **Time reference**: For a steak about 2 cm thick, searing both sides over medium-high heat for a total of 4 minutes yields medium-rare. Adjust by about 1 minute per 1 cm increase or decrease in thickness.  
   - **Note**: Adjust doneness based on the cut—fillet suits rare to medium-rare, sirloin about medium-rare to medium, and ribeye up to medium; generally, avoid going beyond medium.  
6. **Rest the Steak**  
   - Transfer the seared steak to a warm plate or cutting board, loosely cover with aluminum foil, and let it rest.  
   - Resting time is roughly equal to the cooking time.  
   - **Note**: Do not cut into the steak during resting, or the juices will run out.  

## Health Advice  
Steak is rich in high-quality protein and iron, making it suitable for muscle building and blood replenishment. However, control the amount of butter to avoid excess calories. Pairing with a vegetable salad improves nutritional balance. Beyond medium doneness, the steak’s tenderness may be compromised.

# Dream Power Hokkaido Milk Toast  
Cuisine: Other  
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: All Seasons  

## Ingredients & Seasonings  
🥖 Bread flour 500g (main dough)  
🍞 Bread flour 50g (for tangzhong, about 30g used per loaf)  
🍬 Granulated sugar 50g  
🥛 Milk powder 15g  
🍯 Honey 25g  
🥛 Milk 350g  
🧈 Butter 50g (softened in advance)  
🍚 Active dry yeast 5g  
🧂 Salt 5g  
💧 Water 150g (for tangzhong)  

## Utensils & Equipment  
⚖️ Digital scale  
🥣 Large stainless steel mixing bowl  
🧊 Refrigerator  
🍳 Stand mixer (optional, for kneading)  
🧤 Gloves  
🍲 Small saucepan  
🥄 Spatula  
🛡️ Plastic wrap  
🌡️ Thermometer  
🥖 Rolling pin  
📦 Loaf pan (450g size)  
🔥 Oven  
🔫 Digital thermometer  
🧇 Cooling rack  
🧤 Oven mitts  
📏 Ruler  

## Instructions  
1. Prepare the refrigerated tangzhong: Mix bread flour, granulated sugar, milk powder, honey, and milk until combined with no dry flour. No need to knead. Cover with plastic wrap and refrigerate for 1 hour or overnight.  
   **Key point**: The long rest allows the flour to develop gluten naturally and effectively lowers the dough temperature, which benefits subsequent kneading.  

2. Prepare the tangzhong: Bring water to a boil in a saucepan, then turn off the heat. Immediately add bread flour and stir quickly until a smooth paste forms. Let cool before use.  
   **Key point**: The tangzhong gelatinizes the starch, significantly improving the bread’s moisture retention and softness.  

3. Combine the main dough: Remove the refrigerated tangzhong from the fridge. Add the cooled tangzhong and mix well. Then add dry yeast and continue kneading until the dough reaches the stretchy stage.  
   **Note**: The dough will be sticky when the tangzhong is first added; this is normal—just keep kneading.  

4. Use the delayed salt-and-butter method to knead until full gluten development: Once the dough reaches the stretchy stage, add salt and softened butter. Continue kneading until a thin, smooth, translucent membrane forms (windowpane test). For hand kneading, add butter after about 10 minutes of kneading, then knead for another 4–5 minutes. If using a stand mixer, use speed 4.  

5. First fermentation: Round the dough, place it in a lightly oiled bowl, cover with plastic wrap, and let it ferment at about 28°C until doubled in size.  
   **Doneness check**: Dust your finger with flour and poke a hole in the dough. If the hole doesn’t spring back or collapse, fermentation is complete. Avoid over-fermenting.  

6. Divide and rest: Remove the fermented dough, divide it into portions of about 245g each. Round each portion, cover with plastic wrap, and let rest at room temperature for 25 minutes.  

7. Shaping: Gently press the dough to release air. Roll it out into an oval shape with a rolling pin. Flip it over and roll it up. After a short rest, roll it out again into a long tongue shape. Flip it over and roll it into a tight cylinder, using your fingertips to seal the edges neatly. Place two dough cylinders side by side in the same direction into the loaf pan.  

8. Second fermentation: Let the dough ferment at 36–38°C until it reaches 80% of the pan’s height.  
   **Key point**: Reaching 80% fullness is critical for a well-shaped loaf after baking. Under-fermentation will result in a less square shape.  

9. Baking: Cover the loaf pan with its lid, place it into a preheated oven, and bake at 190°C for 30 minutes. Adjust temperature or time as needed based on your oven’s characteristics.  

10. Demolding and cooling: Immediately after baking, tap the pan sharply on a countertop to release steam, then turn the bread out onto a cooling rack. Let it cool completely before slicing.  
    **Note**: Tapping and quick demolding prevent the bread from collapsing, deforming, or trapping steam.  

## Health Advice  
This bread uses tangzhong and water roux techniques to greatly enhance moisture retention. Its calories come mainly from the dough and butter, making it suitable for replenishing energy after exercise. However, those with blood sugar sensitivity should consume it in moderation.

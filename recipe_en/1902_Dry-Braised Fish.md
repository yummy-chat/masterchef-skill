# Dry-Braised Fish
Cuisine: Sichuan  
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Season: All Year Round  

## Ingredients & Seasonings  
🐟 1 carp (about 750g; can be substituted with other fish)  
🥩 120g pork belly (fattier is more fragrant)  
🧄 35g garlic (about 6–7 cloves, diced)  
🫚 30g ginger (diced)  
🧅 30g scallions (cut into large sections; to be removed after braising)  
🌶️ 35g Pixian doubanjiang (broad bean paste, minced into a paste)  
🫒 Cooking oil, as needed (for greasing the pan, frying the fish, and stir-frying the ingredients)  
🍶 10g huangjiu (1 tbsp)  
🍶 10g light soy sauce (1 tbsp)  
🍶 3g dark soy sauce (1/3 tbsp, for color)  
🍶 25g aged vinegar (20g added during seasoning, 5g drizzled just before serving)  
🍬 20g white sugar (2 level tbsp)  
💧 250g water (1 bowl)  

## Kitchenware & Equipment  
🍳 Wok (iron wok)  
✂️ Kitchen scissors  
🔪 Chef’s knife and chopping board  
🥄 Spoons (for scaling, drizzling sauce, and measuring seasonings)  
🧻 Paper towels  
🍽️ Plate (for serving the fish)  
🥢 Slotted spoon or ladle (for pouring oil and basting sauce)  
🥢 Chopsticks (for removing scallion sections)  
🥣 Bowl (for measuring water)  

## Cooking Steps  
1. **Prepare the fish**: Use a spoon to scrape off the scales and slime from the fish body. Cut off the fins and gills. Rinse the blood along the backbone under running water. Trim the tail into a swallowtail shape.  
   **Note**: The blood along the backbone must be thoroughly cleaned; this is essential for removing fishy odor.

2. **Dry the fish**: Use paper towels to pat the fish dry inside and out, to prevent oil splatter and sticking when frying.

3. **Score the fish**: Make diagonal cuts on the back of the fish, spaced about one finger width apart, cutting only into the flesh without hitting the bone. Then make a second row of cuts in the opposite direction to create a crosshatch pattern. Do not cut into the belly.  
   **Key point**: Avoid cutting through the bone, or the fish may break apart during frying.

4. **Prepare the aromatics**: Dice the pork belly, garlic, and ginger. Cut the scallions into sections. **The Pixian doubanjiang must be minced into a fine paste** to release the red oil properly.

5. **Prevent sticking for frying**: Add a generous amount of oil to the wok. Heat over high heat, swirling the oil to coat the pan for about 3 minutes until smoking. Pour out most of the oil, leaving a bit more than usual for stir-frying.

6. **Fry the fish**: Gently slide the fish into the wok. Cook over medium-high heat without moving it for about ten seconds, then shake the wok to ensure even heating of the head and tail. Fry one side for about 40 seconds, then flip and fry until both sides are golden brown, about 1.5–2 minutes total. Remove the fish and discard the oil used for frying.

7. **Stir-fry the minced pork and base**: Add a slightly generous amount of fresh oil to the wok. Add the diced pork belly and stir-fry over medium-high heat until it changes color. Reduce to medium-low heat and continue to stir-fry until the pork renders fat, shrinks, and turns slightly crispy. **Reduce heat to the lowest setting**, add the minced doubanjiang, and stir-fry until red oil and fragrance are released.

8. **Bloom the aromatics**: Add the scallions, ginger, and garlic. Turn the heat to high and stir-fry until fragrant.

9. **Prepare the sauce**: Splash huangjiu and light soy sauce along the edge of the wok. Stir-fry a few times. Add one bowl of water and bring to a boil over high heat. Add dark soy sauce, 20g aged vinegar, and sugar. Stir to combine.

10. **Braise**: Place the fried fish into the sauce. Reduce to medium heat. **Keep the wok uncovered the entire time**, and continuously spoon the sauce over the fish. Cook for 7–8 minutes, until the sauce thickens and clings to the fish. Shake the wok occasionally to prevent sticking. Once the scallion sections have loosened, remove them with chopsticks.

11. **Reduce sauce and serve**: When the sauce has reduced until the oil separates from the liquid and only red oil remains, turn off the heat. Carefully transfer the fish to a serving plate using two spatulas. Turn the heat to high again and cook the sauce in the wok for a moment to thicken further. Drizzle in the remaining 5g aged vinegar, stir well, and immediately pour over the fish.  
    **Key point**: Adding vinegar just before serving greatly enhances the vinegar aroma. The sauce should be reduced until only oil remains in the plate—no watery liquid—to achieve the dry-braised effect.

## Health Advice  
This dish is rich in high-quality protein from fish and has a moderate fat content. The pork belly and doubanjiang add flavor and calories, making it a good occasional indulgence. Note that doubanjiang and light soy sauce are high in salt; those with high blood pressure should reduce sauce intake. For weight loss, omit the pork belly and add tender tofu to enhance freshness.

## Preparation Steps
1.  Place the old hen, pork skin, pork leg bones, scallion, and ginger in clean water. Bring to a boil over high heat, skimming off any foam. Reduce to low heat, cover, and simmer for 2 hours. Remove all meat and bones, crush them with the back of a spoon, return to the broth, and boil vigorously over high heat for 1 hour. Finally, strain out all solids to obtain 18 jin (approx. 9 kg) of stock.
**Key point**: Slow simmering tenderizes the meat, while vigorous boiling extracts essence into the broth, turning it white. If stock is less than 18 jin, add water to the meat residue and simmer to supplement.

2.  Weigh all dried spices, place in a container, soak in warm water for 20 minutes, then drain.
**Key point**: Warm water soaking removes impurities and releases aromas.

3.  Heat a wok, pour in 900g vegetable oil. Add ginger slices, onion, scallion, garlic cloves, and cilantro. Fry over medium-low heat until caramelized and dry. Remove the solids to obtain seasoned oil.
**Key point**: This is for the initial new brine; in subsequent batches, the oil primarily comes from rendered fat from the meats.

4.  Lightly oil the wok, leave a small amount of oil, add 60g granulated sugar. Stir-fry over medium-low heat until the sugar melts. When the sugar turns from white to yellow to reddish-brown and forms fine bubbles, turn off the heat. Let the large bubbles burst using residual heat, then quickly pour in the prepared stock and stir well to create caramel coloring.
**Key point**: Stir constantly. The bursting of large bubbles signals the time to add stock. Act quickly to prevent the caramel from turning bitter.

5.  Into the 18 jin of stock, add the soaked spice sachet, salt, chicken powder, rock sugar, first-class soy sauce, MSG, cooking wine, prepared caramel color, scallion, ginger, oyster sauce, Super Fresh No. 1, yeast extract, ethyl maltol, fish sauce, chicken juice, white pepper powder, and all other seasonings. Finally, pour in 900g of seasoned oil. Bring to a vigorous boil for 5 minutes, then turn off the heat and let the brine rest ("wake up") for 24 hours.
**Key point**: The resting period allows the spices and brine to fully meld. The total weight of this first batch of brine is approximately 21 jin.

6.  Bring the rested brine to a boil. First, add pig ears and cook for 30 minutes. Then add pig tongue and cook for 25 minutes. Finally, add the chicken carcass and cook for 20 minutes. Once all items are cooked, turn off the heat, cover with a weighted rack, and let soak for 1 hour before removing.
**Key point**: This batch is primarily to enhance the flavor and aroma, converting the new brine into an aged brine. Maintain the lowest possible heat ("steeping brine"), ensuring ingredients are submerged.

7.  Remove all meats and the spice sachet. Use a fine mesh strainer to remove any solid residue from the bottom of the brine. Wipe the inner walls of the stockpot clean with a damp cloth. Allow the brine to rest overnight in a cool, ventilated area.
**Key point**: Brine maintenance is crucial to prevent spoilage.

8.  Soak the cleaned free-range chicken in clean water for 2-3 hours to remove blood. Prepare the marinade according to the proportions. Place the drained chicken in a vacuum tumbler, add the marinade, and tumble under vacuum for 2 hours. Alternatively, marinate by soaking. After marinating, discard the liquid and thoroughly drain the liquid from the chicken's cavity.
**Key point**: Do not pour the marinade liquid into the brine, as it will affect the flavor and preservation.

9.  Position the marinated chicken breast-side up. Insert the left wing from behind the neck, securing the wing's middle joint with the chicken's beak. Fold the other wing back. Use a knife or cleaver to break the leg bone on the inside of the thigh. Tuck both chicken feet into the abdominal cavity, folding the leg bones back so the chicken sits in a curled, nest-like shape.
10. Before the official chicken brining: a. Skim off the surface oil from the brine, reserving 50g per jin of brine. Store the excess for later use. b. Adjust the total volume of the degreased brine back to 18 jin by removing one-tenth of the aged brine and replacing it with an equal amount of fresh water. c. Heat the cycled brine until it just begins to bubble ("seemingly boiling, but not quite"), skimming off any foam. d. To the 18 jin of base brine, add 72g salt, 36g MSG, 36g chicken bouillon, 18g rock sugar, 4.5g ethyl maltol, 18g Baijiu, 36g ginger slices, along with 900g of the reserved seasoned oil. Finally, add one new spice sachet.
11. Place the trussed whole chickens into the re-seasoned and boiling brine, ensuring they are fully submerged. Bring to a vigorous boil, then immediately reduce to the lowest possible heat, maintaining a barely open surface with minimal bubbles ("steeping brine"). Cook for 2 hours. Place a stainless steel rack or weight on top to prevent the chickens from floating.
**Key point**: Do not stir the chickens immediately after placing them in the pot. Reduce heat immediately after the brine returns to a boil. Maintain the absolute minimum heat; only tiny bubbles should be visible on the surface.

12. After 2 hours of steeping, turn off the heat and let the chickens soak for another 30 minutes. Carefully remove and drain the broth. Prepare a clean, large wok or pot and line the bottom with a layer of white granulated sugar. Place a steaming rack inside, arrange the drained chickens on it, and cover tightly with a lid. Smoke over high heat for exactly 4 minutes. Turn off the heat and let it sit, covered, for another 1 minute. Immediately remove the lid and take out the smoked chickens.
**Key point**: Use high heat throughout and time accurately. White smoke appears in the first 2 minutes; the smoke turns yellow in the 3rd-4th minute. Use the smoke color to gauge the process. Remove the lid immediately after smoking.

13. Immediately after smoking, use a brush to coat the chicken skin evenly with the reserved seasoned oil (from step 10a). Place the smoked chickens in a cool, ventilated area to cool down naturally and "rest." The meat will tighten, and the flavor will concentrate. Serve after cooling.
**Note**: Do not bag or wrap the chicken while it is still hot, as the skin will become soft and bitter.

## Health Advice
This smoked chicken is rich in high-quality protein and collagen, suitable for replenishing energy and for those concerned with beauty. However, note its high salt and fat content; those with high blood pressure, cholesterol, or blood sugar should consume in moderation. Control the amount of sugar during smoking to avoid the formation of potentially harmful substances from burnt caramel.

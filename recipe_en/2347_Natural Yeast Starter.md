# Natural Yeast Starter  
Cuisine: Common Home Cooking  
Difficulty: ⭐⭐⭐  
Seasonal Recommendation: Suitable for all seasons  

## Ingredients & Seasonings  
🌾 Unbleached whole wheat flour 10g (for initial use; add proportionally for subsequent feedings)  
💧 Cool boiled water / filtered water / sunned tap water 10g (for initial use, equal weight to flour)  

## Kitchen Tools & Equipment  
🫙 Glass jar or glass cup (transparent for easy observation)  
🥄 Spoon or chopsticks  
🪢 Rubber band (to mark initial batter height)  
⚖️ Kitchen scale  
🔥 Microwave / oven / near a hot water kettle (to provide warm fermentation environment)  
📦 Plastic container with lid (for freezing storage)  

## Instructions  

### Preparation Phase  
1. **Choose flour**: Use unbleached whole wheat flour. Its bran and germ are rich in nutrients and natural microorganisms, helping cultivate the yeast culture.  
   **Key point**: Bleaching kills the natural bacteria in wheat; avoid bleached flour.  
2. **Prepare water**: Use cool boiled water, filtered water, or let tap water sit for a day before use to remove chlorine.  
   **Key point**: Chlorine has sterilizing properties that inhibit yeast growth.  

### Phase 1: Cultivate Yeast & Inhibit Contaminants  
1. **Day 1**: Place 10g whole wheat flour and 10g water in a clean container. Stir thoroughly until well combined. Let it ferment for 24 hours in a warm place.  
   **Key point**: Cover the container with a plate to keep out dust, but do not seal. If the ambient temperature is low, place the container near a hot water kettle or inside a turned-off microwave for warmth.  
2. **Day 2**: Observe the batter—small bubbles may appear but no significant rise; the smell should be normal. Directly add 5g water and 5g whole wheat flour, stir well. Continue fermenting for another 24 hours.  
   **Key point**: The first feeding is small (1:1 ratio) to initiate fermentation.  
3. **Day 3**: More fine bubbles appear, with a mild sour and musty odor. Add 10g water and 10g whole wheat flour, stir well. Continue fermenting.  
   **Key point**: Feeding amount increases; do not yet track height changes.  
4. **Day 4**: Fermentation changes become noticeable; the odor strengthens. Remove 10g of the starter, add 10g water and 10g whole wheat flour, stir well, then transfer to a clean container.  
   **Key point**: Discard most of the old starter, keeping only a small amount for feeding. This helps control contaminants and provides sufficient nutrients.  
5. **Day 5**: The sour smell intensifies, but the musty odor significantly diminishes. Remove 10g of the starter, add 10g water and 10g whole wheat flour, stir well.  
   **Key point**: Lactic acid bacteria multiply rapidly; the acidic environment effectively inhibits contaminants, fundamentally changing the microbial population.  
6. **Day 6**: A fresh sour smell is noticeable, with no musty odor. Remove 10g of the starter, add 10g water and 10g whole wheat flour, stir well, then transfer to a clean cup. Use a rubber band to mark the initial liquid level on the outside of the cup.  
   **Key point**: From now on, gauge yeast activity by the height change at the rubber band mark. Start collecting discarded old starter for later use.  

### Phase 2: Enhance Yeast Activity  
1. **Observation & Feeding**: Observe whether the yeast expands to about double the rubber band mark within 7–12 hours. Once it rises to this point, proceed with the next feeding.  
   **Key point**: The core factors affecting activity are maintaining appropriate temperature and preventing the yeast from starving—feed it promptly when nutrients are depleted.  
2. **Stable Feeding**: Once the yeast consistently doubles in size within the expected time, feed it using a 1:2:2 ratio (starter: flour: water) twice daily (morning and evening).  
   **Key point**: The 1:2:2 ratio is suitable for overnight feeding, preventing the yeast from running out of nutrients during the night.  

### Long-Term Storage & Revival  
1. **Freezing**: Mix in a 1:1:1 ratio (starter: water: flour), stir well, place in a sealed plastic container, and freeze.  
2. **Revival**: Remove from the freezer 1–2 days before use and thaw at room temperature. Once thawed, it can be used directly, or feed it once at a 1:1:1 ratio and wait until it rises to restore full activity.  

## Health Advice  
This homemade natural yeast starter is rich in probiotics and dietary fiber, making it ideal for bread enthusiasts seeking a healthy flavor. During the initial cultivation, be sure to use unbleached flour and control the water temperature to prevent excessive growth of contaminants.
